  5. [S565] Source not known, JOSIAH COLLINS Among the original settlers of Lexington, Kentucky, were two brothers Elijah and Josiah Collins. In August 1780, Stephen, whose name appears among the earliest lot holders then a resident of Bowman's Station when returning from the successful expedition of General George Rogers Clark against the Indian town of old Chillicothe (three miles north of the present city of Xenia, Green County, Ohio) and Piqua stopped in Lexington and exchanged the horse he rode for a 3 acre lot of growing corn, a cow, a few hogs and some farming utensils. He also exchanged his cabin at Bowman's Station for one in Lexington into which he removed permanently in a few weeks. His bargain was considered a bad one, and his change of home dangerous for the capture on the 22nd of June previous, of Ruddles and Martins Stations on the waters of South Sicking, had left Bryan's Station and Martin's and Lexington the most exposed position in the country. Mr. Collins consoled himself with the remark that it was as well to die by the sword as by famine - the latter being threatened south of the Kentucky River, by the wild game having been hunted out or driven off. Soon after, the young corn and pumpkins were available for food and as the corn grew hard the mortar and pestle turned it into hominy and meal for bread. The skins of deer and elk when dressed, furnished clothing; stretched on a frame with wooly side up, the buffalo hides made at once bed and bedstead, and no warmer covering could be desired than the hides reversed with wooly side down. Shirts were made of the linen manufactured from the lint from the bark of the dead nettle stalk.
    COLLINS' WILL In the name of God Amen. I, Josiah Collins being in good health and sound mind and memory do publish this my last will and Testament hereby revoking all former wills made by me at any time heretofore made as to such worldly estate as it has pleased God to intrust me with. I dispose of the same as follows: First I direct that my just debts and Funeral Expenses be paid out of the first money that comes into the hands of Executor to be paid out of my personal or monied Estate. Second I will to my oldest Son Willie Collins One dollar. Likewise I will to two younger Sons Fielding & Bohannon Collins one dollar each. I will my fifty acres of land that George Moore lives on to Elizabeth Moore's children. That is to say one acre to be laid off in the South West corner of said fifty acres and the same to be equally divided amongst Elizabeth Moores four Sons. All the other part I will to Jennetta Moore not with standing I send the above fifty acres of Land to my daughter Elizabeth Moore during her natural life and at her death to descend to her children. As above I further say that George Moore, husband to Elizabeth Moore, is not to have any part in the land directly nor indirectly. I also will to Elizabeth Moore two hundred dollars to be lent out during her natural life and the interest to be paid annually by my Executor to Elizabeth during her natural life and at her death to be divided amongst her children. I will to Perlina Moore the money that remains of my estate at my death to be lent out on interest during her Perlina Moore's natural life by my Executor and the interest paid to said Perlina annually so long as she may live and at her death the principle to descend to her children. It is to be understood plainly that the money willed to Elizabeth and Perlina Moore is to remain in the hands of my Executor during the life of the above Elizabeth and Perlina Moore and to be lent as above. I hereby make and Ordain Bohannon Collins my Executor to this my last will and testament written with my hand on two sides of one half sheet of paper. I hereunto set my hand and seal this 30th day of Sept. 1845. Josiah Collins (seal) Bath County April Court 1848.
    This instrument of writing purporting to be the last will and testament of Josiah Collins deceased, was produced in open court and proven by the oath of D. B. Boyd to be in the entire hand write of the testator and the same was ordered to be recorded where for the same has duly admitted to record in my office as the Law directs. Given under my hand as Clerk of the County Court for the County aforesaid this 13th day of April 1848. Wm. M. Sudduth, Clk. A copy Attest Cole Barnes, Clerk. Bath County Court, Ky.
    Josiah Collins died about 1847, In the census of pensioners of Revolutionary and Military service of 1840 published 1841, Josiah Collins age 83 was living with Silas Moore, his son-in-law in Owingsville, Bath Co. Kentucky. Rev. & 1812 Department of the Interior Bureau of Pensions Washington, Feb., 21, 1924. Edgar R. Harlan, Curator, Historical Dept. of Iowa Des Moines, Iowa Sir: I have to advise you that from the papers in the Revolutionary War pension claim, S. 30336, it appears that Josiah Collins was born May 2, 1757, and that in Feb. or Mar. 1778 while on his way to Kentucky from Halifax Co. Virginia, where he was born and raised, he enlisted in Captain Dillards Company. Also on its way to Kentucky and served therein for three months. He then enlisted and served under Captain Harrod until Feb. 1779, when he joined Captain Holder's Company and served until Apr. 15, 1779. He then entered a command under Lieutenant Patterson and aided in building a blockhouse where Lexington now stands, where he remained until May, 1779, when he volunteered under Colonel John Bowman for an expedition against the Indians with whom they had battle at Chillicothe after returning from this expedition he continued on duty at the blockhouse until August 1, 1779. On Apr. 1, 1780, he returned to said blockhouse and was on duty there until Sep. 1780, when he went to guard the fort at Bryan's Station. In October, 1780, he went out under Colonel Daniel Boone against the Indians. He was in battle at Bryan's Station Aug. 1782. On Nov. 1, 1782, he volunteered in Captain McConnels' Co. under Gen. George Rogers Clark and aided in destroying five Indian towns on the Miami River. On Nov. 30, 1782 he left the service. He was allowed a pension on his application executed May 13, 1833 while living in Bath Co. Kentucky. There is no data on file as to his family. Respectfully, Washington Gardner Commissioner.
