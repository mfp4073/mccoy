Dewitt Miles Benham

M, b. 8 September 1862
     Dewitt Miles Benham was born on 8 September 1862 at Marysville, Yuba Co., California. He was the son of Dewitt Clinton Benham and Cynthia Arne. Note.1

Citations

  1. [S455] The National Cyclopaedia of American Biography, pages 115 - 116. DeWitt Miles Benham. BENHAM, DeWitt Miles, clergyman, was born at Marysville, Cal., Sept. 8, 1862. His family settled in New England prior to the war of the revolution. His great-grandfather, James Benham, made his home at St. Albans, VT, and in 1784 was married to Sarah Sedgwick, a cousin of Noah Webster. James Benham afterward removed to Bridgeport, N.Y., dying there in 1807. The family then removed to Penn Yan, N.Y., where DeWitt Clinton Benham, the father of the subject of this sketch, was born. He left home at an early age, traveled extensively in the South, and attracted by the gold discoveries went to California over the Mexican route in 1849. In 1861 he married Cynthia Arne of Auburn, N.Y. Having returned East in 1865, he became interested in coal operation in Beaver county, Pa. Here his son, DeWitt M. Benham, received his academic training, and then entered Westminster College, where he was graduated with honors in 1883. The young man taught Latin and Greek for a year in Jefferson Academy, Canonsburg, Pa., then entered the Western Theological Seminary, and in 1886 was licensed to preach the Gospel by the Shenango presbytery. Having entered Union Theological Seminary in New York city, he was graduated the following year. On Oct. 13, 1887, he was ordained at West Lebanon by the presbytery of Kittanning, having accepted a call to the Second Presbyterian Church of Kittanning and the church of Appleby Manor. Within two years he received a call to the Point Breeze Presbyterian Church of Pittsburgh, and on June 1, 1889, was installed as its first pastor. He resigned this charge in 1893, and began new work in the east end of the city. The result was the organization of the Tabernacle Presbyterian Church, Feb. 21, 1894. Services were held in Masonic Hall, until a suitable building was secured on Collins avenue and Station street. The Tabernacle now numbers some 600 communicant members, and is one of the influential churches of the city.
