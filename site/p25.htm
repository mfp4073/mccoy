  1. [S375] Letter from Johnnie Bogue to Margaret Speaker dated 17 April 1968, page 1.
  2. [S121] Richard Bruchman newspaper article. An Indian trader for nearly 75 years, R. M. Bruchman, now 94, still goes to work every day. He works perpetually. He has been just as conscientious in years of service to the city of Winslow, as he has served as a member of the Winslow Housing Authority since its foundation in 1954, and was a member of the war Housing Board, clear back to 1942. He has resigned from that position recently, and his grandson Phillip, has taken his place on the board. He is being recognized by the city of Winslow for his many endeavors and Mayor James L. Curtis, and the city council have declared Friday February 25, R. M. Bruchman Day. Richard Maxwell Bruchman, was born 27, November, 1882 in London, England. His parents were German, who migrated first to England, then sailed for the United States, when only 10 days old. They settled in Ripon, Wisconsin, where his father Otto Bruchman, and wife Louise, ran a tailoring shop, raising four boys; Oscar, Richard, Arthur, and William, Bruchman. Richard remained home until at the age of 17, was attracted to the southwest to Gallup, New Mexico, then to Arizona. "There is no other place," he insists. He came to Winslow in 1902 and found work with Babbitt Brothers as a clothing clerk in their store on Front Street. A short time later, he bought the Indian Trading Post, from Mr. McAdams, in 1903, near the Colorado River, about 10 miles from Winslow, on the Navajo Indian Reservation. Here he remained for 18 years, with his wife, Cora Lynn Bocklett, of Gallup, New Mexico. There was six children born to this couple, five boys and one girl. Four died in infancy. In 1920, Bruchman moved to town and opened his first Indian Curio Store. His grand-daughter, Donna Harris, and grandson Phillip, are working with him. His grand-daughter Linda Parson, works for the Culligan Water Co., and goes to the NAU, in Flagstaff. His youngest grandson works for a scientific company in Flagstaff, Arizona. He is recognized as an authority on Indian blankets, silverware, and jewelry. He speaks English, German, Navajo and Spanish, fluently. He was the author of several articles on the Navajo Indians that were published in the Winslow Mail. He has served as president of the Winslow Rotary Club and president of the Chamber of Commerce, as well as continuing the work on the Housing Authority. The Mayor and council in proclaiming R. M. Bruchman Day, charged and encouraged all citizens of Winslow to enter whole heartily into the spirit and good purpose of the observance.
