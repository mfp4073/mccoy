  5. [S630] Newspaper article about the death of Francis A. Galligan. The Daily Mail, Francis A. Galligan. The people of Hagerstown were stunned when they picked up their newspapers yesterday morning to read of the sudden death during the night of Francis A. Galligan, a man who, in the five short years that he made this city his home, had done more for the city of his adoption than perhaps any other single individual in recent years. Hagerstown got only fleeting glimpses of this busy man whose tremendous energies and genius in first conceiving, then in working out the most minute details with his many contacts with the federal government, made possible the expanded Fairchild industry here, which is already giving several thousand persons employment and will give work to many hundreds more in the very near future. Mr. Galligan was a happy but tired man when he left the ceremonies that dedicated the new Fairchild unit on Saturday evening, August 23. A few hours later he was to enter the local hospital for an appendectomy and a much needed rest. The operation successful, he apparently was recovering rapidly and was planning to leave the hospital this week to resume the task he considered the most important in his entire career--that of feeding to Uncle Sam the trainer airplanes which are so sorely needed in training the boys who may soon be called upon to defend this country. Only an hour before he was suddenly stricken, he had discussed with his associates his plans for the future of this rapidly growing industry in Hagerstown. Francis Galligan gave his life for his country, say his associates, for it was his almost incredible energy and persistence in building for Hagerstown its No. 1 industry in a few years, that undoubtedly had undermined him physically. Mr. Galligan became interested in airplanes as a flying cadet during the World War. That war over he decided to make the building of better airplanes his life's work. He has been connected with the airplane industry ever since. Coming to Hagerstown in 1936, he became head of the Fairchild plant, then just one of the group of smaller diversified industries here. Things began to happen when he took the reins, which was to culminate with the completion and dedication of one of the most modern airplane manufacturing building in the country only two weeks ago. Over two thousand persons have already been given employment here, five or six times as many as were employed when Mr. Galligan first took charge. And the development of this industry here as conceived by this genius is just the beginning of even greater things for Hagerstown. There was a love for the man who worked beside him in accomplishing his plans that revealed itself on rare occasion which endeared him to those who helped make what he had done possible. His employees recalled only two weeks ago when a group walked into his office with a beautiful American flag which they had purchased themselves to fly over the new plant, that their boss was deeply touched by their loyalty. Then there was the occasion a year ago when he lost by death his only son, and his employees had collected a sizeable sum with which to purchase flowers, that he asked them to give the money to some worthy charity. Hagerstown and the airplane industry is going to miss Francis Galligan. The place of this adopted son in this community is going to be difficult to fill.

He laid down his mortal for an immortal body, calmly and peacefully as if sinking into a gentle slumber. a fitting change for a life so nearly perfect. E.A.B.

  3. [S648] Obituary of Thomas F. Croft. Los Angeles Times, Pasadena. CITY LOSES ITS FOUNDER. Thomas F. Croft Dies After Useful Life. PASADENA, May 5--The death of Thomas F. Croft late yesterday afternoon, takes from Pasadena its founder, the man who at one time owned the land on which the city is now located and who organized the famous Indiana Society, the first settlers of the Crown City. Since December 12, 1873 he was a resident. He was the first white man to live on what is now Pasadena and his life was devoted to upbuilding one of the most delightful residential cities in the world. He was the organizer of the famous Pasadena Pioneer Society, the records of which form a comprehensive history of Southern California. He was born in Painesville, O., April 30, 1834, where he was educated in an academy and afterwards in a business college. In 1854 he came to California. For six years he was manager for Wells, Fargo & Co., at Downieville, Cal. In 1862 President Lincoln appointed him paymaster in the United States Navy. He was sent to New Orleans and detailed with Rear-Admiral S.P. Lee, where he served until the end of the war. He then returned to Ohio, and later went to Springfield, Ill., where he was cashier in the Mad River National Bank until 1873. Early in the fall of 1873 he came to Los Angeles to make his home in Southern California. In looking about for an investment he decided that the Rancho San Pasquel, then comprising the entire mesa from the San Gabriel Mountains to the valley lands along the big wash, was a most desirable location for the establishment of a colony of eastern people who might be looking for a place for a winter residence. In the meantime he met D. M. Berry and J. M. Baker, who had been sent to Southern California by a number of people in Indianapolis, to look for the most desirable climate for winter residences. At that time the Rancho San Pasquel belonged to John S. Griffin and B. D. Wilson. Judge Benjamin F. Eaton, who died in Los Angeles last fall was agent for the owners. Croft proposed to buy out Griffin's share in the ranch but Wilson objected. After much dickering the deal was made and Croft bought Griffin's interests amounting to 3900(?) acres for a consideration of $25,000, paying $6250 in cash and giving his note for $18,750. This transaction took place in December 12 of that year. In the meantime he negotiated a trade with the representatives of the Indiana Colony and two months later turned the entire tract over to that colony, which had organized under the name of the San Gabriel Orange Grove Association. Under the articles of agreement the entire tract was divided equally among the members of the colony. Mr. Croft received as his share, sixty acres, which land is now in the heart of Pasadena. He was made secretary and treasurer of the association, which position he held for many years. Mr. Croft never engaged in any active business outside of his position with the society and looking after his own interests, which were large. Three years ago he became afflicted with slow paralysis, which finally caused his death. Until a couple of weeks before his death, he retained control of his mental faculties. Mr. Croft was a charter member of the Pasadena Presbyterian Church. He was one of the organizers and a faithful member of Godfrey Post, G.A.R. He was a subscriber to the first issue of the Los Angeles Times and took the paper every day since that time. In I$$$(?) he was married to Miss Eurietta A. Wallace, who survives him. There are no children. The funeral will be held at the undertaking parlors of W. H. Pierce & Co., on North Lincoln avenue, at 2:30 o'clock Saturday afternoon.

  8. [S653] The Past on Parade. The Daily Breeze, PASADENA - Pasadena's Indiana Colony was settled not long after the Civil War. One of the settlers was Thomas Finley Croft, pictured here in 1864, who had served as an assistant paymaster in the U.S. Navy from 1860 to 1865. The late Museum of History volunteer, Mary Borgerding, wrote short bios of all the founders. She titled Croft's, "Money, Mules and Matrimony." She also wrote that he was on the Monitor when it battled the Merrimac and suffered a broken eardrum from concussion of the guns. A better title might have been, "The Man Who Bought Pasadena." A group of Indianans calling themselves the California Colony of Indiana in 1873 had sent representatives west to locate and buy property. After looking at many sites, and hooking up with early settler and water supply expert, Benjamin Eaton, the west side of the San Pasqual Rancho was chosen. They couldn't agree on price and boundaries with the owners, Dr. John Griffin and Benjamin D. Wilson. Another problem was that a financial decline caused some of the Indianans to pull out of the venture. Thomas Croft showed up after a sea voyage and took over negotiations with the help of Judge Eaton. They also recruited more investors. His diary says, "Went down to Wilmington to see Mr. Wilson about San Pasqual Ranch and am convinced that he will do anything that is right. Met Gen'l. Banning there. Am pleased with him. Judge Eaton is in town and with me." Croft got all the legal papers made out and on Dec. 26 paid $25,000, $6250 cash and $18,750 in notes. This was for all the land from Fair Oaks to the Arroyo Seco, from South Pasadena to the mountains. Three days later, he transferred the deed to the San Gabriel Orange Grove Association. Croft ended up with 60 acres west of Fair Oaks to the Arroyo and from California to Palmetto. Other settlers wrote that Croft with his mules did the first plowing in the colony. Croft hitched his mules to a wagon and took young people on moonlight rides. On one ride he met and later married a Canadian girl, Eurietta Wallace. Croft continued ranching and was treasurer of the Orange Grove Association until 1881. He died in 1910. Sid Gally is a Pasadena Museum of History volunteer.

    Thomas Croft was an organized man. That served him well during his time in the Union Navy during the Civil War. He was an officer, and he served as paymaster on board a Monitor-class ship in the fighting around Mobile Bay. This then-new class of ship, based on the famous Monitor, was an armored, turreted vessel designed for fighting fortifications and other ships. It was low to the water, hard to hit, and had a small number of very large cannons on board. In battle, part of Croft's assignment would have been to carry any messages from the captain to the gun crews. In those pre-electronic days, only a voice tube connected the captain's post to the gun turrets, and that was unreliable in combat. Unfortunately, in those pre-ear protection days, the men serving those enclosed guns often suffered varying amounts of hearing loss. This was true of Thomas Croft, who eventually lost much of his hearing.

    A few years after the war, Croft became part of a business venture to buy land in California. His personality and paymaster experience left him as the one trusted to handle the money and the financial paperwork. The group wanted to buy enough land to create an agricultural community. Croft came west and began negotiations with Benjamin Wilson and John Griffin to buy enough land for their project, but the group didn't have quite enough money to make the purchase. So, Croft talked former Colonel Jabez Banbury into joining the group, as well as ex-Confederate Aurelius Hutton. Their extra money was enough to put the deal on track.

    Because of the nature of real estate law, and the vague business arrangements involved, the entire piece of property was signed over to the individual making the deal, and the sellers left it up to Croft to straighten things out at the receiving end. Thus, for roughly a day, Thomas Croft owned all of what would become Pasadena.
    Once the rest of the group came west, they divided up the land and laid out the first roads. Their meeting to divide the land was held on a rise just west of where Pasadena Museum of History stands today.

    Croft chose a piece of land at what is now the corner of California and Orange Grove, and he is credited with being the first one in the colony to begin the process of planting. The group would soon plant many fruit trees and grapevines.

    Within a year, Croft helped found the local Presbyterian church, becoming one of the trustees. Mrs. Croft apparently thought that their new home was a little drab, and she felt homesick. So, Croft planted a garden of roses, the flower for which Pasadena would soon become famous. His may have been the first such garden on south Orange Grove, a short walk from where the Tournament of Roses headquarters now stands.
    He invested in various businesses, including the Pasadena Packing Company, which shipped fruit from this area to other parts of the country.

    The rest of the Croft story isn't as happy. His hearing loss garbled the stories he told of his past, so that even the newspaper articles mentioning him had facts wrong. Some listed him as having served on the Monitor, rather than a Monitor-class ship, and lauded him for his battle experience, but listed him in the wrong battles.
    Near the end of his life, Croft displayed signs of what might today be called a form of dementia, perhaps Alzheimer's. As a result, he was ordered to be housed at Patton Asylum in 1910, but he died at his home before he could be sent there.

    The question next arose, "Why doesn't Will Barnes mention a brother in his autobiography, Apaches & Longhornes, published by the Ward Richie Press in 1941 and reprinted by the University of Arizona Press in 1982?" The answer appears to lay in an unintentional oversight on the part of Frank C. Lockwood, who edited the autobiography for publication, and a lack of careful attention by subsequent historians. In his introduction to the 1941 edition, Lockwood--an English professor at the University of Arizona--enthused that "I undertake the editing of this volume as a labor of love and, also, as a service to the State of Arizona, to which I acknowledge a deep devotion, as did also the writer of these memoirs." He went on to explain how "during his later years Will Barnes spent much time in the preparation of his autobiography, writing down with racy and graphic detail the more exciting and worthwhile incidents of his remarkable life. Indeed, the record was so full and extended that it has seemed to Mrs, Barnes and me that it would gain in unity and would appear to been advantage in book form if limited to an account of the author's Arizona experiences." Lockwood, therefore, omitted the first 172 pages of the manuscript autobiography, in which Barnes describes his youth and young manhood, that includes references to his younger brother, Tom. Lockwood mentions only that after the death of his father, Enos Rollin Barnes, in 1863, Will "and his widowed mother went to make their home at LaPorte, Indiana, with his grandfather Croft," leaving the reader to assume that Will was an only child. In fact, Will and Tom lived with their mother in Indiana, and for awhile on their uncle's farm in Minnesota, until Tom struck out on his own in December of 1876.

Barnes, indicates a high degree of conformance between the two." Scheips apparently refers only to the portions of the reminiscence that Lockwood actually used in Apaches & Longhornes, In the same note, Scheips describes Thomas F.
