  4. [S213] Newspaper article from the Scrapbook/Album maintained by Caroline S. Hastings, page 17. Death of Mrs. Hastings, of Clinton. We chronicled yesterday the death of Mrs. Dr. Seth Hastings, of Clinton, but had not time to pay our passing but heartfelt tribute to her worth. She was the sister of Hon. Thomas E. Clark, of this city, and her residence in the county reaches back nearly if not quite to her infancy. Her husband is well known to all the inhabitants of Oneida county as one of the oldest and most honored physicians in our whole bounds.
    Mrs. Hastings was a woman of cultivation, of pleasing though quiet manners, of noble generosity and pure religion. She reared a numerous family to manhood and womanhood in the paths of strict virtue, from which, it is not too much to say, they have never departed. Her children can indeed rise up and call her blessed. Some of them are respected clergymen, and one of them is a missionary to the island of Ceylon. Her loss is not confined to her family. The community are with them a common sufferer. They will miss her kindness, her example and her hospitality. We remember with gratitude the warmth with which in years that are past she welcomed the stranger and the friend to her genial hearth. There is not a doubt of her final rest.
  5. [S207] Francis H. Hastings, Family Record of Dr. Seth Hastings Senior of Clinton New York, page 27D and 27E. Huldah Clark Hastings, Wife of Dr. Seth Hastings.      She was the daughter of John and Ann Emmons Clark being fifth in a family of seven. She was born in Colchester County, May 14, 1783, and moved with her father's family in 1797 to Clinton, Oneida County, N. Y.
         She married September 12, 1802, at the age of 19 years Dr. Seth Hastings. About 1808 they moved into the red brick house still known as the "Hastings Homestead" (in Clinton) and here 12 of her 15 children were born and here she died of typhoid fever September 21, 1850.
         In person she was a tall, large framed woman of dark complexion. Her large soft brown eyes gave her her only claim to actual beauty, but her fine expression of kindness and goodness lent a peculiar grace to her countenance. She was a woman of noble character steadfast in her devotion to husband and children and very faithful in the performance of every religious duty. Even the smallest child realized that "Mother" was not to be interrupted when she retired for a time each morning to the "front room" for a season of private prayer. No matter what was claiming her time and attention in her large household she never neglected this privilege and when she came again into the busy life of the house, it was clear to all that she had received strength and help from God. It was to daily seeking of Divine guidance and the consecration of her naturally strong will, that she attributed her power of self-restraint even under sever provocation. Her naturally quick temper was so controlled that few of her intimate friends even suspected that her calm judgment and gentle charity for the failings of others were graces given to one who had faithfully striven to overcome her own heart.
         During these early years of her married life her husband's young brothers, whose house was about two miles south of Clinton, made their elder brother's house their second home, and so from the very first there were always many coming and going from the two families, both Hastings and Clarks and Parmelees with the later connections of the Bradley's, Judds, Fooks, etc.
         As the Hastings brothers settled in different places, and had growing families, they arranged a systematic exchange of children in order to give each child the discipline and advantage of being away from his own home people. In this way nearly everyone of the nephews and nieces were brought under her motherly care and influence. The elder grandchildren, too, were often sent "home" in times of illness in their own homes, and all were made welcome. Besides doing for each of these near members of the allied families there was a large hospitality exercised toward all strangers and neighbors who claimed it. Many a time in later years her sons recalled with amusement their boyish reluctance to care for the horses of the ministers and lecturers who always made the doctor's house their headquarters while visiting Clinton.
         But it was at the Commencement time at Hamilton College that the resources of the "Brick house" were taxed to the utmost. The great brick oven was hardly large enough to supply food enough for over Sunday for the many guests who came in from each session at the church in addition to those who were staying in the home. In those days there was no cooked nor canned food to be bought and so the Monday morning of Commencement week came none too soon for the replenishment of the larder, and the final departure of the guests sometimes left the family well tired out. Usually several relatives who came to attend Commencement stayed for some weeks and there was always a large family to be provided for.
         Mrs. Hastings herself always arose during these busy summer days at 4 A. M. and in the quiet hours before breakfast she planned the day's work. Each daughter was trained to be a skillful housewife and their assistance with that of one maid under her direction served to keep order in the house and to provide for the large family.
         It was after an unusually busy summer that Mrs. Hastings was induced to attend the meeting of the Board of Foreign Missions at Oswego. Upon her return she complained of great fatigue and before many days a severe form of typhoid fever had developed and the end came only a few days later.
         Her husband never recovered from the grief and shock of her death and a few months later he was suddenly stricken with paralysis. For more than 10 years he was helpless and was cared for with the greatest devotion by his two maiden daughters, Elizabeth and Caroline.
