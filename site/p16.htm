Then in 1880 went west to Socorro and Carthage new Mexico where Alexander Cunningham worked for Alexander Bowie, his brother-in-law, who was prospecting and locating coal mines for the A.T. & S.F. Rail Roads. And was located at Carthage New Mexico. While out in the field he caught cold and Pneumonia developed. After five days at home he passed away, leaving his wife and six children. Then Alex Bowie and John Stewart an uncle by marriage went to Gallup New Mexico where they located more coal mines. Then William Bowie and Margaret Robinson Bowie, mother and father of Alex Bowie, and Margaret Bowie Cunningham moved to Gallup New Mexico and Margaret Cunningham and children came with them where they opened a small dry goods store and Emma Bowie a sister a milliner store in the same building.

  6. [S349] Obituary of Mary Cunningham. MISS MARY CUNNINGHAM PASSES TO HER REWARD The gentle spirit of Miss Mary Cunningham passed from this earth Monday, January 17, 1927. Death came as relief from many years of invalidism which had been borne with fortitude and resignation. Her soul had been tried in the fire of suffering and now released from its earthly tenement finds rest and relief in an invisible world. Miss Cunningham was a native of Pennsylvania and came to New Mexico with her family when but a girl. They first lived at Carthage, New Mexico, she removing to Gallup with them in 1885. Here she grew to womanhood. Here, with the exception of a few years, the greater part of her life has been spent. Here her eyes closed for the last time upon earthly scenes. The deceased had made her home with Mrs. Sam Wood, her sister, and in the years of her affliction has always received the tenderest care. For the past eight years she has been unable to walk but she never suffered for anything that loving hands or medical skill could provide. Three sisters survive, Mrs. Murray B. Johnston, of Roseville, Calif., Mrs Henry L. Benham, of Williams, Arizona and Mrs. Sam Wood of Gallup. She was a niece of Alex Bowie, a Gallup pioneer, who was one of the first to develop the Gallup coal fields. The funeral service was held at the Gallup Undertaking Parlors, on Jan. 19, 2:30 p. m., conducted by Rev. Elfring, of the Congregational church. Many beautiful flowers covered the casket, tokens of love and grief. Burial was in Hillcrest cemetery.

The services were conducted by Rev. Osborn. He succumbed to the attack of appendicitis before an operation could be performed. Mr. Cunningham was twenty one (two) years old and unmarried. The remains will be shipped here for burial and the funeral will take place Monday. Mr. Cunningham was a young man of excellent character and made friends with all whom he come in contact. The DEMOCRAT extends its sympathy to the bereaved sisters and members of the family. (Sept. 25, 1902).
