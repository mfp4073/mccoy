  5. [S519] Obituary of Caroline F. Michaelis. LIFE ENDED FOR VENERABLE LADY Mrs. Michaelis Was in Her 82nd Year. LIVED HERE MANY YEARS. After Long Period of Activity as Help-Meet of German Lutheran Minister, at His Death She Returns to Her Old Home Where Remainder of Life is Spent--Funeral on Friday. The passing of one of the good and noble women of Crawford county is noted in the death of Mrs. Caroline F. Michaelis, widow of the late Rev. A.G.H. Michaelis, which occurred Tuesday night at 10:40 o'clock at her residence, 214 South Walnut street, this city. The deceased was aged 81 years, four months and four day. Death was due to infirmities incident to old age and followed an illness extending over a period of almost two years during which she gradually declined. Possessed of a rugged constitution and an indomitable will, she remained active in her various duties until Friday of last week when she was forced to take to her bed, eventually succumbing to the inevitable. Unconscious during the last few hours, she passed peacefully away in the presence of four of her children and other relatives and friends who had been drawn to her bedside, Caroline Fredericka Michaelis, nee Marggaf, was born Sep. 2, 1832, at Stadt-Ulm in Schwartzburg, Rudelstadt, Germany, where she was also baptized. At the age of five years she emigrated with her parents, Mr. and Mrs. Henry Marggraf, and a sister, Augusta, on May 31, 1837, to America. The family at once located in Bucyrus where her father for a time followed the occupation of shoemaker. She spent her girlhood years in this city and here was confirmed in the faith of her German Lutheran church, of which she remained a consistent and devoted member throughout the rest of her life. She was confirmed by Rev. John Krause. On Nov. 20, 1853, she was united in Marriage with Rev. A.G. Michaelis, then pastor at Arlington, Hancock county, to which place she removed. Later she moved with her husband to Bucyrus where the latter served as pastor of the German Lutheran church from 1855 to 1867, during which time the old brick church was built on Poplar street. Later she made her home in Liverpool, Ohio, Monroe, Mich., Union City, Ind., and Westerville, Ohio, at all of which places her husband held pastorates. Following his death at Westerville in 1885, she returned to the scenes of her girlhood and has since made her home in the old homestead in this city. To the above union were born eight children of whom three preceded her in death--Theodore H., August H.W. and Hugo O. Surviving children are Mrs. Martha L. Reichert of Mansfield, Ernest R., of Spokane, Wash.; Mrs. John H. Sheetz, New Washington; Oscar E., of Kendallville, Ind. and Lorenz P., of Bucyrus, county surveyor of Crawford county. She is also survived by one sister, Mrs. Sophia Smith of this city, and one brother, Charles Marggraf, of Lykens township, besides other relatives. A marked characteristic of Mrs. Michaelis was her disposition to take the happiness with the sadness of life as it came, without feeling that fate was unkind to her when ill winds blew. Her disposition was one of serenity, though she was ambitious and very active, even in her advanced years. She was a thoroughly good woman of the old fashioned ways, pleasant as a companion, modest in her tastes and thankful for life's blessings and philosophical toward it griefs. For over 30 years she worked by her husband in his labors in the ministry, reared her children to be useful men and women and dwelt in amity with her family and her friends always. After her husband's death she lived quietly at her home here, her son Lorenz being her devoted companion during the closing years of her life. Though she is gone, her memory will every remain with her children--the memory of a mother who was all that the best of mothers could be, and a worthy Christian woman. The funeral will be held Friday afternoon, brief services being held at the residence at 1:30, followed by services at Good Hope Lutheran church at 2 o'clock, Rev. O.A. Bertram officiating. Burial in Oakwood cemetery.

  4. [S516] Obituary of Oscar Edward Michaelis. Kendallville News-Sun, ACTIVE CAREER OF O.E. MICHAELIS ENDED MONDAY. Had Been Identified With the City's Official Family for Past Six Years. DEATH CAME FOLLOWING NERVOUS BREAKDOWN. Was Former Newspaper Publisher and Prominent Vocalist; Funeral Is Thursday. Oscar Edward Michaelis, city clerk and former well known prominent newspaper publisher, died Monday afternoon at 5 o'clock at the East Haven hospital at Richmond, where he had been taking treatment for several weeks. The news of his demise came distinctly as a painful shock to his many friends. His death followed a general breakdown due to overwork. Monday morning he developed a high temperature caused by cerebral irritation and failed rapidly until the end came at 5 o'clock. According to the attending physician he was conscious to the last. The physician had sent a telegram early Monday morning to Mrs. Michaelis, hoping the message of his alarming condition would reach her prior to his death, but owing to yesterday being a holiday, the telegram did not reach her until after he had expired, as telegraph offices were closed a portion of the day. For some time Mr. Michaelis had been in poor health, although his condition was not regarded as critical. He went to the hospital at East Haven, June 24, as a voluntary patient, realizing that he was in need of treatment and decided to seek the same at the state hospital at East Haven, where he had been so materially benefited in the winter of 1918-19. Oscar E. Michaelis was born June 23, 1868, in Liverpool township, Medina county, Ohio. He came to Kendallville February 1, 1907, as owner, with Jacob Geiselman, of the Kendallville News, and was instrumental in 1912 in consolidating the News and Sun, the two daily papers in the city at that time. On account of failing health he sold his interest in the Kendallville News-Sun to the late C.O. Merica, Following the death of William P. Myers on July 12, 1914, he was appointed city clerk and held that position ever since, being re-elected at the last city election. He was also secretary of the Kendallville war chest committee and took active part in all lines of war relief work. He was married January 31, 1900, at Marion, Ind., to Miss Capitola McClure. He was formerly city editor of the Logansport Reporter and also Logansport Pharos. From 1895 to 1900 he was a singer with the Al G. Fields, W.S. Cleveland Hi Henry minstrel shows and had sung in practically every city of 5,000 and over in the United States. For the past ten years he had sung in the Presbyterian church choir here. He was a member of the Presbyterian church, was a Mason and an Elk, being past exalted ruler of the local lodge of Elks and was one of its founders ten years ago. Mr. Michaelis was very prominent in many and varied activities of the city. Politically he was a democrat and by his personal exertions as well as through the medium of his paper, during his period as publisher, exerted great influence in the councils of his party. His career is replete with faithfully and efficiently performed service, and he was held in the high esteem and confidence of those whom he served. Always progressive, he was in hearty sympathy with any movement having for its object the development of the city's resources, possessing a strong and positive trait of character which won for him a high place in public esteem. He was a man of a strong attachment of friends, bearing the truest and deepest affection toward those with whom he associated. His deeds are the best line with which to measure his life and his good works and beneficent influence will make his monument enduring. Surviving besides his wife are two brothers, Lorenz P. Michaelis of Bucyus, O., and Ernest of Spokane, Wash.; two sisters, Mrs. John H. Sheetz of [...w ] Washington, O., and Mrs Martha Reichert of Mansfield, O.; seven nieces and four nephews. Two brother, August and Hugh, preceded him in death. The body will arrive in the city this evening at 6:07 o'clock over the Pennsylvania lines and will be taken to the [...e] home on East William street. A short and informal funeral service will be held at the Presbyterian church Thursday afternoon, Rev. Julis T. Or[...] officiating. Interment, Lake View. Friends may view the remains either Wednesday afternoon or evening.

Michaelis, aged fifty, former Crawford county surveyor, died at his farm home, northwest of Oceola, Tuesday afternoon at five o'clock, after being confined to his bed the past five months. His health failed in October, 1925, and since that time he had been unable to engage in active work. Mr. Michaelis was born in Monroe, Mich., October 5, 1876, where his father, Rev. H.W. He came to Bucyrus when nine years of age, with his mother, formerly Caroline Marggraff, and family, following his fathers' death, who was a former minister here. He attended the schools of Bucyrus, graduating from the high school in 1894, and then attended Case School of Applied Science, at Cleveland, later graduating from the law school of the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, Michigan. For three years he was employed as attorney for the Erie railroad, and returning to Bucyrus, he served two terms as county surveyor. Since that time he had been working as civil engineer in Crawdord and Wyandot counties, and residing on his farm. He was confirmed a member of Good Hope Lutheran church, Bucyrus, by Rev. Mr. Gebhardt, and had always been a faithful member of the church and the Brotherhood of that congregation. He was united in marriage June 22, 1918, to Leona Marie Rowse, who survives, with one daughter, Caroline Elizabeth, aged seven years old. Mrs. John H. Sheets, of New Washington, a sister, and a brother, Ernest of Morgan Hill, Calif., survive. Funeral services will be held Thursday; a short service from the home, one mile north, and one mile west, of Oceola, at one o'clock, and from Good Hope Lutheran church at two o'clock, Rev. O.A. In 1873 he took a commercial course at Mt Vernon and after leaving the business school embarked in the dry goods business with his brother under the name and style of J. Sheetz & Brother, which firm existed until 1904. In 1876 he became financially interested in the Farmer's Exchange Bank, taking the position of Cashier, which office he has held up to the present time. Although essentially a business man, and a very successful one, Mr. Sheetz has always taken a close interest in public affairs and has given some of his time to public service. In 1891 he was elected councilman and reelected in 1893. He also served two terms on the School Board and was a member of the congressional convention that nominated Judge Seney on the Democratic ticket On October 2nd, 1889, John H. Sheetz married Thressa W., daughter of Rev. A. H. and Caroline Michaelis, of Bucyrus, Ohio. They have two sons, John A. and Walter F., both of whom are students at the Capital University, Columbus, Ohio. Mr. Sheetz*s father, John A. Sheetz, was born in 1809 in Wittenberg, Germany, and came to America alone at the age of eighteen years. He settled in Columbus in 1827, but later lived at Dayton for a time, finally settling, in 1838, on a timber section in Crawford County where New Washington is now located. There he built a log cabin, cleared a farm and lived until the time of his death, December 7th, 1 889. He was married three times, his third wife, Mrs. Elhregott Hesse, formerly Miss Margaret Donnewirth, being the mother of John H. Sheetz. The Donnewirths are one of the pioneer families of Ohio, which can also be said of the Sheetz family. They came from Alsace, Germany, where Margaret Donnewirth was born in 1813. She came to America with her parents in 1 827 and settled at Canton, Ohio. She died in 1893. John A. Sheetz founded one of the representative families of Crawford County and was a staunch Democrat.
