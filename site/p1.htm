  15. [S485] Personal knowledge of Jim Benham, The following provides a brief biographical sketch of James Benham, better known as Jimmy. It is part of a larger document intended for what would have been his grandchildren. It was primarily written by his son Jim Benham and daughter Barbara Benham Dittmann in the fall of 2009.

    Your grandfather, usually called Jimmy, was born on April 25th, 1915. He was born on the Benham Ranch in Coconino County, just south of the town of Williams Arizona. He was born in the same house that existed until 2007 when it was torn down to build a new house on the same location. It has been told that the house consisted, at that time, of a single room, just the kitchen. Later on a living room and bedrooms were added, and the story goes that Jimmy helped to build the fireplace that was the source of heat for the house, along with the wood burning stove in the kitchen. It is also said that he helped to dig the well and the trench for the sewage system pipe to the ditch across the road. Jimmy grew up on the ranch learning to ride and rope, just like any cowboy.

    Your grandfather, Jimmy, went to school in Williams. For at least one class, he had his mother for a teacher. Surely she did not cut him any slack. For his junior year of high school he was sent to the New Mexico Military Institute in Roswell, New Mexico. There is a picture of him in the N.M.M.I. yearbook, The Bronco, showing him with a bugle. He graduated from Williams High School in June 1932. He went to Arizona State Teachers College, now called the Northern Arizona University (NAU) in Flagstaff and graduated in 1936 with a teaching credential. He played the trumpet in the band. His major was physical sciences with minors in biology and mathematics. We're not quite sure how he met our Mother, Ethel Maxine McCoy, but they both ended up going to school in Flagstaff. It is believed she worked for the same professor as did Jimmy. Ethel was born in Prairie City, Iowa and grew up in Chandler, AZ. She graduated from Chandler High School in 1934. Ethel was two years behind Jimmy in school. Jimmy taught school in Williams, AZ after graduating from college for one year while Ethel continued her studies at A.S.T.C. in Flagstaff. Ethel graduated in 1938. She tried to graduate in 1937 after three years of work rather than the normal four years, but apparently didn't quite make it.

    Ethel and Jimmy were married in Flagstaff on August 14, 1937 and honeymooned at the El Tovar Hotel at the Grand Canyon for a day before going on to Oak Creek Canyon. Jimmy taught sciences, mathematics and music at Camp Verde but did not like teaching. Their first home was in Camp Verde, Arizona. Their grocery bill was about $5.00 per week! Sometime in 1939 Jimmy also attended a Refrigeration and Air Conditioning School in Chicago. He apparently opened a business as a dealer and serviceman in Phoenix for air conditioning. During this time they rented an apartment at 703 E Adams, Phoenix, Arizona. This venture did not work out; Ethel and Jimmy moved back to live with his parents in Williams, AZ.

    Ethel has told the story that she gave Jimmy an ultimatum: move to California or no children. This ultimatum was prompted by the 1939 death in Williams of Mary Virginia, Hank's first wife, as the result of an operation (that probably got infected). The only doctor in Williams was said to have been drunk at the time. Ethel's older sister, Enid, was already living in Los Angeles. She was there during the big earthquake in Long Beach in 1933. Her brother Harry McCoy and his wife Julia lived in Hollywood. Their second daughter, Harriet, was born in Hollywood in 1942. When Jimmy and Ethel came to California they first lived with Harry and Julia (probably at 4456 or 4334 ½ Lockwood St., Hollywood, Calif.) then by at least October 1941 James and Ethel got an apartment of their own at 1210 North Westmoreland, Hollywood, California. By February 1943 they have moved to 1214 ½ North Westmoreland, Hollywood, California. Their first two children, Jim, (short for James who's childhood name was Jay) and Barbara, were born while their parents lived in Hollywood. They were born at The California Hospital in Los Angeles.

    Our parents bought a house at 5915 Tobias Ave. in Van Nuys in late 1943. Jim was almost two and Barbara was nine months old. The house cost $4995.00 with a $500.00 down payment. The house had two bedrooms and one bathroom. Our father built the garage, as did all the other new homeowners. The concrete slab was provided by the builder. Harry was born in November 1948 while our parents lived in Van Nuys. In March of 1956 the family moved to a 3 bedroom 2 bath house at 17506 Lorne St., Northridge, California, cost $14,250. In March of 1959 a lot was purchased on Halstead St. in Northridge for $7,500 with the intention of building a larger house. Plans were drawn up, but the house was never built. The lot remains vacant to this day.

    After coming to California, Jimmy may have attended the Warren School of Aeronautics prior to obtaining employment at Lockheed in Burbank. Harry McCoy did attend this school, got a job a Lockheed and encouraged Jimmy to do the same. Jimmy took a course from the University of California in Tool Manufacturing and Engineering, completing it in January 1945. This was a War Training class.

    After the War Jimmy worked as a washing machine repairman at an appliance store called Sincomb Bros. at 6265 Van Nuys Blvd in Van Nuys. He next worked at Radioplane, in Van Nuys, beginning March 21, 1949. Radioplane made drones for fighter planes to practice shooting down. He retired from Northrop (Radioplane was a division of Northrop) in Newbury Park, CA. There is some thought that he was exposed to radiation on the job, which may have caused his leukemia. His initial job was as an assembler. He worked on a punch press and eventually became a Tool and Die Maker. His last position was at a desk as a Manufacturing Methods Planner. The Parachute Division of Radioplane had the contract to provide the parachutes for the early space capsules. At the end of each flight we would all hold our breath until the capsule was safely in the water. Fortunately the parachutes always worked. He gave Barbara a silver bracelet with a picture of the parachute on it. Katherine may have it now. In August of 1967 Jimmy accepted a job at Weber Aircraft Company in Burbank. The company made aircraft seats. He worked at this job until February of 1970 when he was laid off. It appears he worked next for Lockheed in Burbank from about July 1970 through November 1970. In the months before his death Jimmy was studying computer programming at the Electronic Computer Programming Institute.

    Ethel and Jimmy were members of and attended the First Methodist Church in Van Nuys, CA. They belonged to a group of Tobias residents that played a card game called 500, which persisted even as they moved away. It was a monthly night out for all those with children. Later Ethel and Jimmy joined the Northridge United Methodist Church. Jimmy was active in the Free & Accepted Masons of California, Lodge No. 450. He was also active in the Order of DeMolay with his eldest son. Jimmy belonged to the union and was active in it at least during some periods of his employment.

    When I, Barbara, was in high school, my Mother told me that my Dad was sick with leukemia and would die from it, but no one knew when. He lived for ten years after the diagnosis. In the meanwhile, I went off to college, graduated, got married - my Dad walked me down the aisle - became a teacher and moved to San Rafael. He died on September 17, 1971 of lymphocytic leukemia, just nine months before Katherine was born. He died at the Granada Hills Hospital and is buried at Oakwood Memorial Park in Chatsworth.

  23. [S600] Homesteading of Yesterday, And of Today. Williams News, page 6. After the U.S. Forest Service was created, the forest lands in northern Arizona were withdrawn from homestead entry. Subsequently those who desired to homestead in this area had first to pick the piece of land that was desired and get a fairly accurate description of meats and bounds, preferably with some corner that had been established by U.S. survey. After they had made application, the Forest Service passes on it to determine whether it would be more useful to the nation for farming purposes, or left as forest land. There arose the demand for forest rangers to visit the land and judge whether it was best suited for farming.
         The rangers, at first, were drawn chiefly from cow ranches, and had no uniforms. They were cowboys turned ranger. Another job, and a tough one, that was tossed into the ranger's lap, was that of fighting fires. Rangers also checked on the number of cattle run under permit on the forest land, and the number shipped out. They also had to tally the lumber that was cut.
         The rangers of the early days had no roads to follow to the scenes of their tasks, but were required to find their way through the uncharted woods. It was not an easy task. Sevier, like Benham, found a bit of land that he desired to homestead, and turned to farming.
         Of the hundreds of homesteads taken up in this country few are still in the hands of the original owners, and even fewer still are being actively operated by these owners. One of the exceptions to this is the H. L. Benham homestead, south of Williams. Mr. Benham homesteaded the place in 1912, and still lives there during the summer, and still works the place.
         Benham came to Arizona in 1907 as a ranger for the Forest Service and was employed at Pinedale. He had previously cowboyed for Will C. Barnes, and while engaged in this work had exterminated a lot of prairie dogs. These nervy little animals added greatly to land erosion, and nibbled up more range feed than lots of the livestock. Well, Benham's prowess as a "prairie dog poisoner" became well known, and a major part of his early years with the Forest Service were spend mixing and shipping prairie dog poison for use in Arizona and Colorado.
         In 1909 Benham came to the Williams area, being sent first to Flagstaff because this was headquarters point for the Forest Service at that time.
         Almost as soon as he got off the train at Flagstaff, he was dispatched to fight a fire in Schultz Pass. He was later sent to Camp Clover and worked from this point as a ranger.
         After Benham homesteaded his place he was told by his superiors in the Forest Service he could not live on his homestead and continue to work as ranger, but would have to stay at Camp Clover. With the independent spirit so common among the young men in a big country, Benham promptly quit the government service and went to live on his own place.
         In 1918, he went to work for the Santa Fe Railway, with the intention of helping out until spring-but he didn't terminate his association with the railroad until 30 years and six months later when he retired.
         During this time, although he had hired out as freight cashier, he handled every job connected with the department except baggage man. He filled in as ticket cashier, took over the agent's duties when necessary, and in general proved an efficient and dependable employee whenever needed.
         For 54 years Mr. Benham has had as his companion and chief assistant his wife Katherine, whom he married in Gallup in October of 1909. They reared a family at the old homestead, and recall with alacrity the good times of hunting, dancing, picnicking and various other get-togethers enjoyed by the folks many years ago when transportation and communication weren't as speedy as they are now. Then people got together less often, but when they did gather it was a "high old time".
         Benham has continued to farm his place, and today it is a popular picnic and barbecue site. For more than 45 years he has prepared meat for Odd Fellows barbecues, and for the past seven years the St. John's Episcopal Church has held their annual July 4th barbecue there.
         The Benhams know many an interesting and amusing account of conditions in early Williams, and there's no finer story teller than the genial M. Benham.
         At one time this region was an impressive agricultural center. Early settlers grew bounteous crops of oats, wheat, corn, garden vegetables and certainly potatoes. In the winter of 1890 more than seven tons of "Bill Williams" potatoes were shipped from here.
         Spring Valley, Red Lake Valley and Garland Prairie flourished for years as farming areas. On Oct. 1, 1914 school opened at Garland Prairie with 11 pupils and a Miss James of Prescott as the teacher. She was a graduate of Oberlin College in Ohio. There were more than 20 homesteaders in the Garland Prairie section then.
         The folks used to gather often for dances and parties and each fall held a big barbecue at Red Lake. The blessing of those years was ample rainfall to make possible the dry farming which was carried out successfully without aid of irrigation.
         But came a change-a few dry years through which a farmer might hold out. But it lengthened into too many years with too little rainfall. Finally conditions worsened to send even the most determined farmer to other sections to seek a livelihood.
         The plows were abandoned, the homes vacated, and the school houses made shells of emptiness. The once fertile fields gave way to range growth again and it was left to wild game, sheep and cattle.
         But now a new breed of homesteader has entered the act. This is the summer resident; the two-home family that lives and works in the Phoenix-Scottsdale-Mesa-Tucson metropolitan melee, and relishes a respite in the pine braced air and the mountains.
         The surrounding country bristles with summer home sites-Sherwood Forest, Kaibab Ranch Estates, and Grand Canyon subdivision. Point any direction from Williams and you're zeroed in on a summer home.
         True, you can still drive through Red Lake Valley, Spring Valley and Garland Prairie and see signs of the old homesteads and small sawmills, but also visible are new homes under construction.
         The old farms aren't shuffled into the "gone but not forgotten" category-the land is still here, it's merely been put to other use.

There was no Kaibab Forest district as such then, it was all San Francisco Peaks forest. When he got into the Forest Service office he was asked if he had seen a fire in Schultz Pass, to which he answered "yes." In quick order he was queried as to whether or not he could find the blaze, and relieve the forester already there. He rented a horse from Babbitt's livery stable and right away hit for Schultz Pass and the fire. He relieved the man on duty and spent the next 28 hours fighting fire. He recalls that this is one of the biggest fires he was on during his tenure with the Forest Service. No wonder a little longing for the "good old days" can be detected when Benham tells of his Forest Service duties, and hunting trips of many years ago. He says that he and his wife Katherine used to cover a district that comprised 14 townships. They'd travel horseback, and take along pack horses or mules, or sometimes make the trip in a spring wagon. "It was rough," he remarks, "There was very little water lots of times. Some water was not clean, but it was always wet." Many times they would be gone for two or three weeks. Upon spotting one, they would ride down (a 45 minutes trip) report the fire and set out to find it, through their knowledge of the lay of the land. There weren't nearly so many fires because people weren't crashing around all through the woods like they are now. Most fires were lightening caused. Seldom did cowpunchers or sheepherders leave campfires untended, knowing the devastation of a forest fire. "Hunting was lots more fun then," the white haired, senior citizen added. We would form a hunting party, take a wagon, and plenty of feed for the horses. Sometimes we'd be gone two weeks and never move camp, or never see any other hunters except those in our own bunch. We could kill three deer, and there was no limit on turkey. The hunting season was open from mid-September to mid-December. He united with the Presbyterian Church at an early age, and ever maintained a consistent, unblamable Christian life. In 1861 he was elected deacon in the Pres. Year after year from the time of his early manhood, he was regularly in his place at Sabbath worship, in Sabbath School, and the weekly prayer meeting; and for many years in Penn Yan, in Rushford and in Angelica, his voice led the choir in the service of song. It was in the every-day home life that Mr. Benhams' character shone brightest. He was the kindest of neighbors, the most affectionate of parents, the most thoughtful of husbands. His wife was a daughter of Dr. Laurens Hull, and for her, through all the varied experiences of their married life, Mr. Benham evinced a tender love and profound respect, which are somewhat rare and always beautiful to witness. He followed her beloved form to its last resting place in Nov. 1879, and two years later he went to Oakland, California, to spend the closing days of his life with his eldest son. A.M. Benham. She gave directions for the disposition of her effects, for her funeral, and for her family, after she should be gone; and all so calmly that it did not seem like a death-scene. There was neither trepidation nor ecstasy. Her preparation for this event had been made long before. She had often expressed the wish that her death might be like her mother's. This wish was granted. Having left farewell messages to absent ones, and taken leave of those who stood about her, she closed her eyes and gently "fell on sleep." Besides her husband, she leaves two sons; Mr. A.M.
