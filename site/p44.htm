  11. [S728] Civil War Information for Samuel T. Gilbert, The following paragraphs contain some of the more interesting information contained in some 90 pages of Samuel T. Gilbert's pension file from service in the Civil War. Generally, it contains requests for an increase to his pension, surgeon's reports, affidavits from friends and neighbors that Samuel had no "vicious habits" and other correspondence. Samuel's initial claim for a disability pension included rheumatism and varicose veins. Later, he lists blindness of left eye and disease of eyes, heart disease, sciatic nerve problems and finally a general "brake-down" of his health and old age. It is interesting that initially Samuel spells his wife's given name as Rebecka. But by 1915 he was spelling it Rebecca. Also note that his daughter, Martha M. Gilbert Wilson, who took care of him in his last 6 weeks of his life is not mentioned in either list of his children that he enumerates on two separate occasions. The following information is listed in the order it appears in the pension file rather than strictly chronological.

          Declaration for Original Invalid Pension: On this 6 day of Sept. 1883 Samuel T. Gilbert aged 49 and resident of town of Clarks, county of Merrick, State of Nebraska, enrolled on 24th Oct. 1861 Company D of 22nd Regiment Kentucky Volunteers commanded by Captain Jas. G. Michgan and was honorably discharged at Louisville, Ky on the 20 day of January 1865. Personal description: Age, 50; Height, 5' 10"; Complexion, fair; Hair, brown; Eyes, blue. That while a member of the organization aforesaid, in the service and in the line of duty on the march from Alexandria in the State of Louisiana on or about the first day of June 1864 he got up one morning with the balls of his feet hurting him very much. He could scarcely walk. The surgeon said it was inflammatory rheumatism. I frequently had cramps till they ruptured blood vessels. The hurting in my feet has continued ever since, especially in summer.

          War Department April 21, 1884: Samuel T. Gilbert, a Private of Company "D", 22 Reg't was enrolled on 24 Oct. 1861 at Grayson, Ky. for 3 years and is reported: On muster rolls from enrollment to Aug. 31/62 present.
          Sept. and Oct. 1862 absent sick. Nov. and Dec. 1862 absent without leave, same to Feb. 28/63; name not borne on subsequent rolls to Dec. 31/63.
          Jany & Feb. 1864 present, with remark; to forfeit 15 mos. pay and make good the time lost (twelve months).
          March & April 1864 not on file. May and June 1864 present.

  6. [S59] Obituary of John B. Gilbert. John B. Gilbert was born November 27, 1857, at Olive Hill, Kentucky, and died July 4, 1921, at Perry, Iowa, aged sixty three years, six months and seven days. He removed to Prairie City, Iowa, April 17, 1865, where he resided for a number of years. Later he purchased a farm in Guthrie county which he operated successfully for many years. After disposing of his holdings in Guthrie county he resided in Coon Rapids, Iowa. For the past several years he has made his home in Perry, Iowa, where he passed away in the King's Daughter Hospital. The deceased realized that the end was near at hand and talked of his passing without alarm. Although for some reason he had not identified himself with the Church, yet he believed in and respected Christianity. He was a devout student of God's Word, and tried, in an unassuming way, to exemplify the principles of Jesus Christ in all his dealings with his fellow men. He was quiet and retiring, loved by his friends, and held in the highest esteem by his business associates. To the unfortunate and needy he was a kind, constant and liberal friend. In a remarkable degree he had the good will and admiration of the children of his home city, and the news of his severe illness caused them great alarm and his sudden death profound sorrow. He had a passion for the beautiful in nature, and found much pleasure in adorning his home with rare and beautiful flowers. These choice and fragrant blossoms, that have the place of honor on his casket, were his friends in life, and as he would wish it, they are his friends in death. Together they will fade and vanish from view of mortal eyes, to come forth again in the morning of the Spring Day of time and of eternity. Two sisters and two brothers are the immediate survivors who mourn the going of a patient, loyal, loving brother, as follows: Mrs. Martha Durbin of Coon Rapids, Iowa; Miss Amanda Gilbert of Prairie City, Iowa; Alden Gilbert of Wells, Minnesota; and H. Gilbert of Perry, Iowa. The remains were brought to Prairie City, and funeral services were conducted from the Methodist church, Rev. D. J. McKay officiating. Interment in Westview cemetery. CARD OF THANKS: We wish to express our sincere thanks to friends and neighbors for their kindness and helpfulness during our bereavement. Mr. and Mrs. James Durbin. Mr. and Mrs. Harrison Gilbert. Mr. and Mrs. Alden Gilbert. Amanda Gilbert.

[S487] REMEMBRANCES OF BESSIE GILBERT BY HER SON HORACE-- READ AT HER FUNERAL MARCH 10, 1979, In the fall of 1912 a young couple from Iowa accompanied by their infant son left on an eventful trip West in their open touring car. They were motivated to make this rather rash move because this young doctor had become tired of making night calls in the deep snow at below zero temperatures in the Iowa farm countryside. I can truthfully say that this young couple--my father and mother--were not aware that, at that time, there were no roads from Iowa to the West--only wagon trails. As was true of so many young couples of that time and this, they were little concerned with the problems of their lives; they were only concerned with their ambitions and their hopes for the future. About six weeks of adventures too numerous to mention here, they made their way down the then new Apache Trail into Mesa a day or so before Christmas. I have often heard my mother say that she was amazed to see roses blooming as they drove through Mesa. They immediately fell in love with the Salt River Valley and decided to settle there rather than continuing on to California, which was their original plan. During a sight seeing trip around the Valley they visited the newly organized townsite of Chandler. Dr. A. J. Chandler only a few months earlier laid out the town and was then promoting the sale of lots. Dr. Chandler painted a glowing picture of the future of the area and told them of the great winter resort hotel, the San Marcos, which at that time was nothing more than an excavation in the ground. My father and mother decided that this was indeed an opportunity for them and they felt they could be of service to this growing community. My father took the Arizona State Medical Board Examination in January, 1913, and very soon put out his shingle in front of a tent pitched near the spot where they soon built the home they lived in until my mother came to live with me in California in 1970. Life was quite primitive in Chandler in 1913. My parents soon moved out of the tent into a house, but of course, at that time, there was no water system in town. They received water in buckets from a water wagon each morning. There was no electricity, hence they used kerosine lamps for illumination. Their only heating was an old wood burning stove in the living room. Since air conditioning and insulation had not yet been discovered, the summers were miserable in these lightly built homes--but my parents persevered throughout those early years; and soon there was a water system, there was electricity and later insulation and air conditioning. I could tell you many tales of life in Chandler in the early years. In those years came the school system, the early telephones and in 1920 the first paved streets. One of the early institutions in Chandler was the Methodist Church. In the first few months of their residency, my parents, along with other pioneers started this church. To mind comes the names of Appleby and Knox, but there were others. They were all people of determination and vision. I think back on the 65 years since my parents came to Chandler. They endured hardships, but they made a good life for themselves and I am sure they related well to the life of the community during their many active years. Although we are here today to honor my mother, I cannot speak of her without referring to my father for theirs was always a wonderful partnership. Whatever "papa" wanted was always agreeable to my mother. My mother passed away, to the day, 19 years after the death of my father. I recall vividly his services at this same chapel. Following his passing, my mother maintained the family home at 141 N. Colorado Street until in 1970 she was forced to come to California to live with me and my family. My wife, our daughter and I look back fondly on the four years she lived with us until a stroke confined her to a rest home. She was kind; she was gentle; she was patient. She was self sufficient during the entire period she lived with us taking care of her own room and walking up the stairs to our dining room for her meals each and every day with a cheery smile and a good word for all. For those of you who do not understand her walking up stairs rather than down, our home is in the hills and instead of having bedrooms up stairs, ours are down following the contour of the hill. After the tragic stroke took her from us, even in her impaired condition, she continued her gentle ways. Her nurses loved her through the 3 1/2 years she was confined to bed and a wheel chair. Her last nurse daily referred to her as her "doll." She died in her nurse's arms Tuesday, March 6, 1979 as she had lived--gentle to the end.

  12. [S486] Bessie Gilbert's Travels. The Optimist, Bessie Gilbert's Travels. From Curlew, Iowa to Chandler, Arizona in an Automobile. By Mrs. Bessie Sanders Gilbert. Dear Friends, The editor of The Optimist requested me to write an account of my trip from Curlew, Ia to Chandler, Ariz. in our auto. It is a dreadful task to try to recall the many incidents of so long a trip. We traveled about 2500 miles and were about six weeks on the road. We actually spent about three weeks traveling. The other three weeks we spent at hotels where we stopped for repairs and rest. We left Curlew, Ia. at 2 P.M., Saturday, Nov. 2. One of our lady friends wanted to stop at a small town on our way to Stratford. So she and her two small children went with us. We had gone only two miles when we heard a terrible racket. Mrs. Taylor exclaimed "Oh, Doctor! The trunk is dragging." The trunk rack had a flaw in it and had broken and if it had not been for the leather straps the trunk would have been lost, as was our lamp and number. We dumped our trunk into the back seat and journeyed on toward Stratford. About four o'clock we reached the place where Mrs. Taylor stopped to visit friends. At Humboldt, we stopped at Frank Gotch's garage to get a tank of gas for our lights. Mr. Gotch's partner waited on us and seemed a very agreeable gentleman. Later we found that he had switched a searchlight tank for our Presto tank which caused us a lot of trouble, as you will see later. At nine o'clock we reached Dr. Kail's at Stratford and eagerly devoured the belated supper that was waiting for us. Next morning my brother, Garlan, joined us and motored with us to Carlisle. It was a beautiful morning and we had a fine ride until we attempted to ford a creek where a bridge was out. On examination we found we would have to send three miles to Des Moines for repairs. In the meantime we hired a farmer to pull the auto out of the ditch with his team. A drunken sot came out and interfered with us causing a little trouble. Garlan was disgusted and said he was downright glad he was not going with us. We reached Carlisle at 4 P.M. We were terribly hungry, having no dinner. After having our trunk rack repaired next morning we started for Des Moines where our arrangements were completed. There we met Dr. and Mrs. Jordon who had been making a farewell visit to their parents. It was 4 P.M. before we got ready to leave the city. After a long cold ride of 110 miles to Lewis, Iowa (Dr. Jordon's home town.) We were entertained at a home of one of their friends where a fine hot supper was waiting us. I was so stiff from the cold and holding my sleeping baby, I could hardly get out of the auto. Dr. Jordon hustled around to close up his business and early next day, we started on our journey for the golden west. We broke a spring but reached Hamburg, a good sized town, in extreme Southwest Iowa. The hotel had more style than things to eat. Next morning we had our spring repaired. We had gone too far West, so had to return a long way. That night we camped ten miles from St. Joseph. Horace did not like to sleep in a tent so cried quite lustily for an hour or more. Don't know as I blame him much, for the tent was small and somewhat crowded with four people and a baby in it. We cooked our supper by a camp fire, and I can't say I relish cooking that is half dirt. Next morning I was so cold I thought my hands and feet would freeze off. The engine was so cold it took us more than an hour to get it warmed up enough to climb the hill. We went on to St. Joseph, got gas and started for Kansas City. Twice that day we were stuck in the mud because we attempted to cross where bridges were out. We found another spring, and there was a terrific rain, so my impression of Missouri is not pleasant. We reached Kansas City early in the evening, and stayed that night and most of the next day with Mary Harrod Morris, college friend of Mrs. Jordon. Kramer took the auto to the J. I. Case Co. and had it thoroughly overhauled. By four o'clock we were on our way. After motoring for about thirty miles we camped for the night. As we traveled through south-western Iowa, northern Missouri and eastern Kansas, we were impressed with the prosperous homes and elegant farms of the farmers. The homes were simply grand, like you might expect to see in big cities. At Kansas City we took the old Santa Fe trail followed by the Mormons on their trip to Utah. Our faces were sunburned, and when we encountered a sand storm it was quite painful until we put the curtains on the auto. Kansas is noted for its good roads and as the trail followed the railroad it kept us from being lonesome. Traveling through Colorado we saw tons of sugar beets. They do not raise much else out in that country. From La Junta, to Trinidad we were unable to keep the right road as our guide book was not very explicit. We traveled by moonlight until we came to some hay stacks. This looked as though there might be human beings not far distant. You must now remember we were now traveling through a wild unsettled country, towns about every 125 miles. We had only a little water for the baby so the rest of us had to drink sparingly of some grape juice. Washing our hands was out of the question. The next morning it was so cold we did not stop to cook breakfast but we discovered a ranch house about 200 yards away, and learned that the people would have gladly entertained us had they known of our presence. Here we filled our water bag, and learned how to get back to the right road. We hurried on to Trinidad, where we had breakfast and dinner in one at 11 A.M. That afternoon we motored over the Raton pass into New Mexico. Right at the foot of the mountain, is a town called Raton, and we passed under a sign saying "Welcome to New Mexico." The highest elevation we reached, crossing the pass, was 10,000 feet. Most of the time it seemed we were going right down the mountain side. Our hearts went pit-a-pat, and our nostrils filled with the smell of burning rubber, caused by the break bans getting hot, but before many days, we got used to making such perilous descents. In this part of New Mexico the land is all fenced in big pastures, and when we came to the fences, we found trough like bridges for the auto to pass over. We were about to cross one of these bridges when Mrs. Jordon exclaimed "You be sure to hit that bridge" of course we didn't, and a broken spring was the result. We reached the town of Maxwell after dark, secured quarters at a good hotel where we remained until the next afternoon, that we might have our spring mended. About seven the next night we reached Wagon Mound, where we found another dandy hotel. We were certainly surprised to find such fine hotel accommodations, for we thought they would be like the Iowa hotels in small towns. Being tourists, they graciously allowed us to pay the highest prices. As you enter Wagon Mound there is a small mountain that looks like a covered wagon. Right here I began to get a poor opinion of New Mexico. It was just dust, dust, dust. About noon the next day, we reached the hustling town of Las Vegas, where doctor telegraphed for springs to meet us at Albuquerque. We laid in a supply of groceries and journeyed on to Santa Fe. We passed through several Mexican towns, of adobe houses, the citizens were very forbidding in looks. They would rush to the doors and show their white teeth and laugh as if we were freaks. We thought we would do most anything rather than spend the night in one of those towns so we traveled on and would have reached Santa Fe if we had had lights. After the sun went down it got so cold, we thought we would rather sit up in our auto all night than get out and put up our tent. I never spent a more miserable night I think. I had ridden 100 miles that day and worried with the baby, so I was no fit subject to sit in an auto all night, when I was already stiff with cold. I scarcely dared move for fear the baby would wake and cry. It seemed the night would never end, and I surely would die of exhaustion. I would wonder from one minute to the next how much longer I could hold on to the baby. Remember Kramer was crowded behind the driving wheel and had no room to hold the baby. When four o'clock came we climbed out, and built a roaring fire of some dead pines we found on the mountain. Day broke about six o'clock and we journeyed on to through Padurita and Glorieta over crooked roads through the Government reserve to the Montezuma hotel, at Santa Fe. We reached there in time to take our baths before dinner. After diving in our trunks for our best clothes we did not feel any too fine as the hotel was full of swells attending the Scottish rites convention. We had no breakfast, just nibbled some crackers and apples, so the fine dinner of roast turkey, cranberry sauce and ice cream did not go so bad. As I sat there being served by colored waiters, I could hardly believe I had spent such a sleepless uncomfortable night as had just passed, had I not still been so weak and trembling. When dinner was over we took a short nap and went out to see the sights in Santa Fe, the second oldest town in U.S.A. There were Spaniards everywhere. We could not even inquire directions on the streets. Finally I came to a Spanish school girl who could direct us. It really seems a foreign city. We went to the plaza to hear the band concert, after which we visited the oldest church in America, built in 1540. Of course is was adobe, and near it stood a small adobe house said to be the oldest house in America. It looked it. We visited the Palace of the Governors, built in 1600. It is now used for a museum, and contained many interesting relics of the Cliff Dwellers and Indians. On ..... of the bad roads between Santa Fe and Albuquerque, Mrs Jordon and myself took the train. We paid four cents per mile and then were charged 35c for a seat. You pay for every thing you get in this country. Albuquerque is a nice city, but I would not want to live there, as it is bounded on every side by the desert. They had some very nice stores, with Spanish girls as clerks. They have the handsomest depot I ever saw, and an equally handsome hotel, called the Alverado. In Albuquerque we were fortunate in securing two furnished rooms, $5.00 per week. We had a gas range, and all other conveniences of a modern house. This would have been a delightful rest for me, had we not purchased milk at the near by grocery, that had been doped with formaldehyde. Horace drank quite a little of this, and it was not long until he was a very sick child, being unable to keep even water on his stomach. He was very sick for three or four days, until finally the doctor gave him peptonized milk. From then on he recovered rapidly. As soon as Horace was able, we continued our journey, after having spent a week in Albuquerque. We packed our trunks in a box filled up the space with medical books, and had no more trouble from broken springs after we took the trunk off the back of the car. I was quite happy that Sunday morning as we left Albuquerque, the sun was shining brightly, Horace was better, and I was anxious to reach my journeys end. Had I known what was before me I would have been steeped in gloom. The roads were not very well defined, so we soon got on a sand road, which used up our gas, although we did not know it until next morning. We went about 15 miles on this road, before we decided we could not make it that way. Then we went back and met a Mexican who directed us to Socorro. We saw several coyotes but did not take time to molest them but finally one was so very impudent, that Dr. Jordon decided to shoot him and take his hide as a souvenir. He was just climbing out from under the curtains of the auto, with the gun half cocked, and pointed at Kramer, when from some unknown reason, the gun went off shooting him through the leg, splintering the bone in three places. Although I saw it done, I would not realize he was really shot until I heard his yell of pain. Of course, Dr. Jordan felt terrible, but accidents will happen. Remember I now had a sick baby and a wounded husband to look after. Dr. Jordon hastily dressed the wound and we started on in hope of reaching a hotel, where we could make Kramer more comfortable. Dr. Jordon now had to run the car, he was not used to running a Case car so it was very awkward for him, and we could make very little progress, however he did exceedingly well considering the experience he had. Very soon we had a blow out, we had no inner tubs repaired, so my husband between groans tried to vulcanize some. Mrs. Jordon assisted Fred in removing the tire, while I sat in the dusty road, trying to rock Horace to sleep. I will never forget that scene as the moon came up, our teeth chattering with cold and our stomachs gnawing for food, we did not have the heart to eat supper. Well, after the tube was vulcanized, Dr. and Mrs. Jordon almost wore themselves out trying to pump up the tires, for the pump would not work. After two hours delay, we started on. By-and-by we came to a Mexican house, where we might have staid all night, but we were a little afraid of Mexicans, and we wanted to get better accommodations for the doctor. Very soon the car seemed not to be working right, so we decided to camp for the night. The doctor took a good dose of morphine to ease his pain. When morning came, and we were ready to start, on examining the tank, discovered we were out of gasoline. We thought there must be a town a short distance ahead, so Dr. Jordon said he would go on and have some gas sent out. As our water bag was about empty, Mrs. Jordon and I walked back hoping to get it filled. We met a Mexican and his family with a barrel of water. The gestures we made trying to make them understand that we wanted water, must have been amusing. Noon came, then night, and no Dr. Jordon. Not until seven the next morning did we hear lagging foot steps. He had walked 20 miles the day before finding neither people nor houses. It was five o'clock before he (Dr. Jordon) came to a ranch house only to find no body at home. He quenched his thirst at a water tank and entertained serious thoughts of catching a chicken and devouring it raw. The chickens had gone to roost so he was afraid to put his hands in for fear he might get caught in a trap so he starved it out. He made friends with the dogs and tried to sleep with them but finding this too cold walked on five miles to some hay stacks where he staid until three o'clock the next morning when he started back to camp. He was so weary he had to rest at intervals and his mouth was so dry he could not spit. When he reached camp all we had to give him was some cold fried potatoes we had cooked the night before and were too heartsick to eat. But Horace crawled onto his lap and they devoured them even if they were frozen. You see when we left Albuquerque we expected to reach Socorro by night so did not take a supply for the long stay in the desert. There was nothing for the rest of us to eat, some sugar and butter and cracker crumbs were all we had for Horace. We had to drain water out of the radiator of the engine for him to drink. Mrs. Jordon and I saw at once that we would have to go at once to get some gasoline and food. We walked five miles meeting no body except Indians and Mexicans and, of course, could not talk to them. Here we found a ranch house and an English boy who had stopped to water his horses and he talked for us to the Mexican ranchman. We hired the Mexican to take us to the town of Scholey eleven miles away. I filled the water bag with water and as the English boy was passing by our camp he promised to leave the water bag with the doctors. I also asked him to sell enough crackers to the doctor for the baby until I could get back with provisions. He gave them a box of crackers and about two pounds of cheese and would not take a cent of pay. The boy would have taken us to Scholey but had other pressing business to attend to. I cannot but smile when I think how we looked that day, riding to Scholey with the old fat Mexican, his skinny horses, and rickety old buggy, which I feared would collapse any minute. Mrs. Jordon being rather fleshy insisted that I should ride with the Mexican while she rode on a little box behind. It was about noon when we drove into Scholey – it was only a railroad station, no stores. The young station agent sent the telegram for gas and we had to wait two hours and a half before we got an answer, saying that gas would be sent at once. The agent very kindly sold us some of his groceries and we started for home. It was a long tiresome ride and the sun went down before we reached camp. The dust was fearful and we were shivering with cold as Mrs. Jordon had no coat and mine was very thin. Kramer said "I never beheld such a welcome sight as to see us come riding in with the Mexican." During our absence three autos had passed by. They sold the boys gas and gave them plenty to eat, but as they had seen no signs of us ladies the doctors were very much alarmed. We had gone off on a side road to reach this little town of Scholey, but our husbands were afraid something had happened to us in such a wild country. They wanted to go on with the other autos but, of course, could not go without us. That night about eight o'clock a chauffeur from the garage in Socorro brought us gas, having come 65 miles. It cost us $23.00 to get us out of the desert. Next morning as we were just ready to start two big automobiles from Chicago and bound for the coast came along. We joined that party as we had found it did not pay to travel alone. We had not traveled very far – only a few miles when Mr. McTavish's car was disabled with a broken spindle. They decided to camp there while Mr. Brundage went on to the next town to telegraph for repairs. We followed on, reaching the town of San Antonio. Just before we reached this town we started to ford the Rio Grande river, had we followed our intention none of us would have been alive to tell the tale. Then we went back and they told us we had to cross on the railroad bridge. The next day was Thanksgiving day and we had a fine turkey dinner. Kramer got only some scraps that night for supper, for he had to go back that morning to bring in Mrs. McTavish, Mrs. Brundage and her two children. He was not really able to go, for his leg was very painful, but we felt we must do something to help them out of their difficulty. We staid in this Mexican town about a week waiting for repairs to come for Mr. McTavish's car. We then drove on to Socorro, Magdalena, and Datil's ranch, in the midst of a snow storm. Dr. Jordon was very ill that night, and we were almost sick with fright lest he might be coming down with typhoid fever. That night after the folks had gone to bed, I decided to go out to the auto for something. The night was very dark and I only took some matches, and just as I struck one, I heard a racket as though there was some one in the auto. I yelled Get out! Ran back to the house and told Kramer there was some body robbing the auto. He limped out the best he could on crutches, while I ran on ahead to tell the landlord to come. When he came with his lantern, all we found was some bacon that had the corner chewed off, and cat tracks over the seat. Right here I want to tell you, we were in a sheep country. We were always offered mutton to eat. I never before had condescended to eat it, but I did now, and found it very good indeed. The next morning, the sun was shining brightly, and the snow melting rapidly. Just as we were ready to start at ten o'clock, a chauffeur from Magdalena driving a Ford car came along, going to Springerville. As Dr. Jordon was still ill we thought he had better go with the Ford man, so as to be sure to get a bed that night. We were heavily loaded and the roads bad, so we thought possibly we might have to camp out. We drove all that day through very beautiful snow covered mountains and large pine trees. Imagine our surprise on meeting three large elephants and two camels on this lonely road. Brundage's dog barked furiously at them. The larger elephant we thought would have attacked us had he been able to budge the other two, which stood between him and our autos. We discovered later that they belonged to a circus that was traveling that way. We staid that night with some Mexicans in the town of Quemado. The boy of the family acted as interpreter and we had very good accommodations. After supper that night Horace got down and tried to play, but it had been so long since he had used his legs, he wobbled like a drunken man. The next day we drove 54 miles over very rough roads to Springerville where we expected to find Dr. Jordon. We reached the town, but could find no trace of him and his wife was nearly frantic. By noon the next day we had heard nothing of him so Mr. Brundage decided to take his car and the doctor, a Mexican guide, and go in search of him. About 5:30 they found Dr. Jordon and the Ford man stranded in the mountains without gas. They had secured some naphtha, but it would not give them power enough to run up hill. Mr. B. and Kramer had plenty of gas but they had their tank full of naphtha, and it would take about two hours to let it run out, so they hitched onto them with a long tow rope and were getting along fine, until they tried to go up a steep hill, where Mr. Brundage broke his car. Then the Ford man drained his car and put in some good gas, while the doctors and Mr. Brundage built up a big fire, as it was 5 below zero and snowing. Kramer, the Ford man, and the Mexican started back to Springerville, but their radiator had frozen and bursted, so they also slept on the mountain side in the snow, ten miles from the other men. The next morning, the chauffeur and Mexican walked to Springerville. The chauffeur took back our car with gas and repairs and all cars returned at 9 o'clock at night with whistles blowing. Kramer had had nothing to eat for twenty four hours, and slept in the snow with only a lap robe for covering. We spent about a week in Springerville, delayed by snow. Dr. and Mrs. Jordon decided to stay here for awhile, and the rest of us with the three cars, left for Phoenix over the White mountains. We traveled about fourteen miles to an old saw mill, camping there for the night. We purchased beef from some cow boys and had some steak and gravy. The cow boys baked some biscuits in an old Dutch oven and treated the crowd. Our gasoline stove was the first these cow boys had ever seen. It was too cold to sleep very much that night, as it was fifteen below zero, and it took us until noon the next day with fires around our autos before we could get them started. One of the boys volunteered to act as guide for us over the mountains, and real glad we were to have him, for the snow hid all traces of the trail. Once he hitched on to our car, and helped us over a steep place just as we were nearing the top of the mountain. Sometimes we had to shovel away the drifts that we might get through. That night we reached Cooley's ranch and camped in one of his houses, cooking our supper on an arm range. Although a wealthy ranchman Mr. Cooley had an Indian squaw for his wife. Next morning we traveled through the noted "Black Canon," but it was frozen and did not cause us the trouble it does some tourists. From here we drove through forest, where trees would measure five feet in diameter and several hundred feet high. About 4 p.m. we reached White River agency on the Apache Reservation. They only asked us 70c per gallon for gasoline. Right in the midst of the town there were several squaws chopping wood. On inquiry we learned they were prisoners and had to do it. Most of the Americans in this town were government employees. We now started for Black River agency, passing Fort Apache at our left. Just about dark we started down a steep mountain at the foot of which was White river. After crossing this we climbed a mountain so steep we could only make about six feet at a pull. We drove up, down, and around most dangerous mountain roads, reached Black River agency about 9 p.m. and camped in a buggy shed off a little store. Next morning squaws arrived with little bundles of hay tied on burros, which they traded for groceries. Mr. Brundage tried to take their pictures, they laughed and hid behind the auto. We spent the day traveling over more rough roads and camped at a government saw mill. Mr. Brundage drained the water from the radiators into a large dish pan, I was busy unpacking, and Kramer was cutting meat for supper, when we heard a scream, and found one of the ladies had set that pan of water on the floor, and the baby had backed into it. First we thought him badly burned as he kept on crying. We applied carbolic acid mixed with Vaseline, and in a short time he quieted down, and ate a good supper. Next morning we had to pay one dollar per car to travel over the reservation. As we passed along we saw many Indians and wondered how they could stand it to stay in their smoking teepees. After driving over more rocky roads, we came to the small town of Rice. Here we saw our first rail road train since having left Magdalena 250 miles behind us, two weeks before. We camped here all night and left in the morning for Copper Globe, a mining town of about 10,000 population. Here we secured a large supply of gas and started for Phoenix. After traveling 40 miles over very good roads, we reached the great Roosevelt dam where we camped for the night. Next morning we took a good look at the dam and departed. Now we had the smoothest road any one ever had the privilege of traveling over. There were mesquite brush on all sides, and cacti as large as trees. As soon as we came to irrigated ranches, we saw all kinds of green things growing which looked good to us after traveling over such barren country so long. While we were stopping in the pretty little town of Mesa, a rancher told the doctor about the new town of Chandler which was just started. We were very much interested and promised to drive over and see it later. We drove 16 miles to Phoenix reaching there after dark. We secured a furnished room for $15.00 the month giving the doctor time to take the state board examination securing him the right to practice medicine in Arizona. Phoenix is a rapidly growing city, and in winter is crowed with tourists. There are many tubercular patients here. We took great enjoyment in visiting the points of interest and were specially pleased to see oranges, grape fruit, almonds, lemons, figs, dates, and olives growing. The school buildings are something grand. On Friday of our first week in Phoenix, we motored to Chandler, we thought it the most beautiful location for a town we had ever seen. We rented a house that was under construction which we purchased later. Chandler is located in the Salt River Valley, surrounded on all sides by mountains. It is watered from the Roosevelt dam, and deep wells. One can hardly imagine this beautiful place one year ago, covered with mesquite brush and cacti and rattle snakes. At present, we have a $150,000 hotel under construction, $8,000 depot, $16,000 school house, and a large bank building. All of these buildings are brick covered with cement. The doctors are building an office, costing $1,550. All of these buildings are being built right now. No, I am not tired of motoring, I would start on a trip this minute if I had a good chance, but no more winter trips for me. Although our trip was hard in many ways, we saw many things of interest, and after finding such a lovely place to live, feel amply repaid. Now friends, if you wish to come to the great golden West to make your home, we will gladly welcome you to Salt River valley. Bessie Sanders Gilbert, Chandler, Arizona.

It is difficult to take a spoken conversation, especially Bill's, and attempt to put it in correct punctuation. In addition, Bill's original emphasis is entirely missing from this printed copy. Bill often corrected his statements, and most of these corrections have not been retained. In many places, the correct spelling of places or names can only be guessed at, a (?), or an alternate spelling, is usually included to indicate this uncertainty. Bill had a habit of using the word "anyway" very often, many of these occurrences have not been retained. His story follows: I was born November 8, 1908 in the very pretty little town of Sausalito in California. I first saw the light of day in a charming home in Sausalito that Dad and Mother had built back in the 1890's. I remember Dad talking about the San Francisco earthquake of 1906 when he was in business in the McCall Building in San Francisco, and literally shaken out of his office. Our home was not particularly damaged except the fireplace bricks came down, the piano was scooted across the room and a few other things to the effect that we did have to go out, the family did that is, go out and live in the wood shed out in back. I think my earliest recollections were of being broke. I sort of grew up in the time of a deep depression when Dad had been building a publishing and periodical business for some length of time, had changed that and had gone into sort of an inventive field where he had invented some type of a pump - a water pump - liquid pump, and also a water filter to fit on the faucet in the kitchen. I can remember he had discovered that the way to hold a portion of that filter together - the only cement that he found that would really stick was melted sulphur, and I can remember in the kitchen of the house melting up pots of this melted sulphur to pour into these containers to purify water, and oh, the stink that it was! Mother didn't care for it very much, but Dad - whatever he wanted to do, he did. There was no ifs, ands or buts from Mother. Dad, before I was born, had some sort of an interest in the Sausalito Water Company, where, at that time, there were springs throughout the hills and piped down to the individual water tanks which were located on everyone's individual property. As a result of that, Dad, way before his time, had pipes all over the roof of our house with holes in those pipes where he could go out and turn on a valve and the entire sprinkler system would then operate. That came to an end in my early youth because we were getting free water from that system and it watered all the garden and the whole place. One day a Mrs. Hickelby(?) , a very wealthy widow who lived across the street from us in a mansion, either came over, or I went over there, I don't know, one of the two, and spilled the beans to the effect that it was a wonderful thing that we could water all of our yard for nothing. Well, it happened to be she was either the owner or the director or the president of the water company, and it was her water that we were using. Actually I think I was a pretty nasty little kid. The immediate neighbors around had children my age, but they had money - the families had money even during the depression times. I remember one time for Christmas, Dad asked me what I wanted for Christmas and times were pretty tight, and I knew they were, whether I wanted a bicycle or a little red wagon. Well I didn't answer that same day, but the next day I thought it all over during the night, and I knew that a bicycle cost quite a lot of money, and that a little red wagon did not. I chose the red wagon. In order to get some money in my own pocket, I guess I was one of the starters of the original garage sale. Out in front of our house was a fairly level place on a corner of the road, and I had gathered up all of my toys or one thing or another that I had, and some of the things that Mother donated, and one thing or another, and I set up a store, ostensibly to raise money. (Telephone rings in background.) You probably just heard the telephone in the background. It was the nurse in the doctor's office telling me that I had to go back to Marin General Hospital for another operation on prostate, which will be on Wednesday, the 23rd at 2:30. I'm not very happy about the entire thing, but I know it has to be done. The streets were not paved then in Sausalito, and somehow or other I had acquired a bicycle, and I can remember riding the bike around the streets of Sausalito along with some of my friends. Sausalito was, and is, mostly hills, and I soon found that a bicycle was of not to great a value when it came down to pushing the darn bike up to the top of the hill again. Finally the great day arrived, I imagine it was around 1920 or 21, when the Sausalito fathers decided, and a bond issue passed, that the streets of Sausalito would be paved. Well the first one to be paved, of course, was Water Street, now called Bridge Way, at the bottom of the hill. And subsequently the other streets were paved of course, to a certain extent they were paved, with what we call a water cure. At that time, (driving a tractor), we took into our home a young man by the name of Stanley Jones - a handsome guy. He was driving one of the construction tractors, and he lived with us, oh I guess, it was all during one summer, until he went back to the University of California for his studies and subsequent graduation. He stayed with us during that time, and also afterwards, when he got a job at the Radio Corporation of America in San Francisco as a stock clerk. I saw him a couple of times. As a matter of fact, the reason I saw him was to go over and try to borrow money for our family to eat on - which I don't know if I got or I didn't. Dad never gave me any real money to use to play with, because he didn't have any. I think at this time he was selling electric cookers, the pre-runner of the electric stove, to the hardware merchants up and down California. I was old enough to drive then, and he bought a new model T Ford Roadster to travel the roads, and I was the chauffeur. Dad could drive, but he would drive the same as he would have driven a team of horses - expecting the car to do exactly what it was supposed to do, without any real help from him. I believe at this time he also had a line of schoolbooks. I think it was called the World Book, that he was selling to the elementary schools and such sales necessitated meeting the Board of Directors and so forth, to get them to buy this World Book. They took the jeep, took my guns, and marched the four of us over through the sword grass underneath a big banyan tree about a half mile away from this road - just a little country road. They sat us down there and said they were going to kill us. O.K. So Noni and I said good-bye to each other. And they posted a sentry on the hill with another guy up there; both with guns on us. But the sword grass was so high that if we ducked down they couldn't see us. We had been there I don't know how many hours. They had taken our jewelry and everything else - wristwatches and rings, and so on. I spoke a little Tagalog and tried to get them to let me go and told them I would be ransom. No. I guess it was about 4 - 5 o'clock in the afternoon, and I could look around and didn't know if the sentry was there or not or if he was sleeping. So I motioned to the Filipino guys I had with me and I said - Come on. So we left the banyan tree, went through the sword grass and got back to the road. Well, just as we got back there, luckily enough, a jeep came along piloted by white boys who recognized we were white and that we were stranded. So they picked us up and took us back to the next Filipino station where we went to a Filipino guard house, told them my story. They paid no attention and didn't give a damn. The usual thing. (Tape space. End of first side, third tape.) She said: "Honey, I can't take it any more - I got to go home - now." She had been out there about a year I guess. My contract was up - or just about. So I talked to the General in charge of Army Grave Registration Service - told him the story and he said, "O.K. - Bill, you're going home." It was about a week later, less than that, I guess, we were on a boat on the old General Pope, on the way back to San Francisco. I was working with - or against I guess - employers who had deducted the money from employees pay checks and failed to turn it in, or failed to file the reports at all. And I had to go out and try to find assets on which to levy, and maybe put them into bankruptcy. To collect this money or get the reports or both. Then I left the office at maybe 9 or 10 in the morning, got in the car and I had a whole bunch of folders. I did, for a couple of months, work pretty good. I did a good job and was commended on it. Well I fouled up, and they pulled me in and started checking on my mileage. Well it didn't add up at all. Well to make a long story short, I don't know how long I worked there - a year - two years I guess - until they said, "Well, Mr. Gilbert, you're through." So I resigned while under investigation. That was that. I lost that job. In the mean time I had borrowed from different lending organizations, and I never paid them back. They were coming down on Noni and this fine job she had. She would go absolutely nuts. Why she didn't leave me I don't know. To this day - I don't know. I got her in more jams than you can think of. We moved a couple of times and we had fun - yeah - we had. But not fun - fun. It was different. Noni was worried of course - all the time. So here I was again without a job. Through my good friend Col. White, who was head of Federal Narcotics Bureau, I got a license as a private investigator. Once again, that was duck soup. I got to work writing out subpoenas, and serving papers and doing investigative work for different attorneys in San Francisco - some of the biggest ones - making some money, but damn little. I was drinking most of the time - drinking it up. By that time, Eric and Irene O'Brien, Noni's mother and father, said "Look you two - you've got to buy a house." Noni was carrying the burden of proof. The Veterans Administration of course paid for the monthly payments on the house while I was incapacitated - I had a mortgage insurance on the house. And so I said - Hell, this isn't making any money - so I think I'll go back in the real estate business. So I took out my license and started advertising other real estate broker's listings - caught hell for that. I made a couple of sales - I don't know. But George Brockman of Brockman Real Estate, who was just starting in business saw my ads. And said - Who is this guy anyhow! He called me up and told me to come on down. I went on down and that began a happy - I guess it was a happy - 10 years or so with him. We built a new building and I was the General Manager - I was getting $500 a month for managing the joint. I was making around $15,000 - $20,000 a year, which in those days was quite good. Drinking all the time. George threatened to fire me two or three times because I was drunk, but anyway - I don't know what happened. Oh yes - George died. He had cancer of the liver or something. And when he died, a lot of me died with him because he was just the same as a son to me. He was younger and a go-getter. The place was taken over by a guy by the name of Allen - who I brought into the Real Estate business and who was a really fast, sharp operator. I couldn't stand working with him. In the first place, he wouldn't stand for the drinking. (Telephone rings in background) So I retired from the Real Estate business. I didn't have any intention of retiring, but anyway they gave me a party, and something or other. I didn't do anything for about a week or two, except drink. Finally joined a very small one or two man office in Mill Valley, named Craymar's. I sold some things down there - made a little money. One way or the other. Then it got too sterile for me. There wasn't enough action at all. Oh it was a deadly thing. And I was drinking all the time. So I left there and went down with Don and Lis Fraser, who had opened a Real Estate business in Mill Valley. And in my more sober moments I made a few sales, made a few bucks. But I was drinking - drinking - drinking. I remember one time I came into the office in the morning, and Don Fraser said - "Bill, you've got to show Mrs. Jones - or whatever her name was - that house this afternoon." I said "What house?" Well this house - and he described it to me - and I said, "And who is Mrs. Jones?" He detailed to me my conversation on the phone with her, and I had no recollection whatsoever of talking to Mrs. Jones, of seeing the house that I had described to her - or anything. It was a complete black-out. This wasn't the first one, but it was the main one. And I thought, Whoa Bill! Wait a minute! So a day or two later, I went up and had a talk with George Kagelman - Kagelman, Seymour & Hamilton - the firm I'm with now. Told them that I drank - they said "Well, as long as you don't drink in the office and don't come in drunk, its O.K. with us." It's an old line firm, such as the Wall Street Journal is in New York. Most of the people in there are quite well to do and a very, very fine office. I was given a big desk and subsequently upstairs with a fire place in the room - there were four desks there. Downstairs was the main part. And they sent me with the proofs to go on down to a printer in Mill Valley to have my cards printed and an announcement of my joining the firm. So I went on down, walked in and met this guy Charlie. Charlie knew that I was a drunk and Charlie himself was a reconstituted drunk. Not recovered, but he wasn't drinking. He had hit a very low spot - everything was gone - he lost his wife, he lost his job, he lost everything - he was out, until somebody brought him to AA - Alcoholics Anonymous. And he had been in Alcoholics Anonymous then about three years. And he recognized in me a fellow traveler - so far as being drunk was concerned. Some woman had actually come into his shop and said, "Look Charlie, you're on AA. Why don't you get that guy in the Real Estate office right across the street over there. He's drunk all the time and bring him in there." Charlie said, "I can't do that. He has to come to us." Make a long story short - Charlie talked to me about AA. Well, I thought, by golly, maybe this was a chance. Now remember, this was the first time - it was my birthday - November 4th or 5th I have forgotten what year it was - four years ago, I guess. It was my first day at work on the new job and I was introduced to Alcoholics Anonymous. Well, I went to a meeting, and I was just open enough to say, Yeah - this just might do it. I recognized my life was completely unmanageable. I was going to walk out and leave Noni - you know everything the drunken insanity does. I started going to meetings and then I started making coffee at the meetings and setting up the chairs. And so forth. Then I finally got to be secretary at one of the meetings. This was naturally after a year or two, and I was imbued with AA - completely. Thought I was. Alcoholics Anonymous is not only a non-drinking deal, but it's a program to live. It's a program for a new life. Wonders never cease. Now its been about four years, and I had one bad slip. First the doctor told me I had to go into the hospital for a prostate operation - O.K. I did. That was just last year - 1979. Came out of the hospital and was home a week or two, and it was Christmas Eve 1979. It was 2 o'clock in the morning. We had been to my mother-in-laws for Christmas dinner. I hadn't been drinking - I was cold sober. About 1 o'clock in the morning I got up and I went into the kitchen to get a drink of water. I didn't turn the lights on. I didn't see my dog, whom I love dearly, in the middle of the living room, and I fell down and knew when I hit the floor that I had broken a hip. So I yelled screaming, bloody murder. Noni got the ambulance and they hauled me up to Marin General. And the next day - or the day after that - they operated on me and put in the usual plates and pins and screws and bolts and whatever the hell they do. I'm just recovering from that now. A week ago I had a lot of trouble going to the bathroom - urinating. The pain was excruciating. So I went back to the same doctor who had done the prostate operation. So he said come back.... He showed me one of them. My God - it was the size of a pea. Well I couldn't take that. It was with a local anesthetic where they poured some juice down the side of the urethra. And he said you'll have to go to the hospital. So that's where it stands now. On the 23rd - Wednesday the 23rd, I go to the hospital for the next operation, and I guess it will be alright. The operation on the bladder was successful, and they pulled out the stones, and I feel better as a result of that.
