Death of Seth Hastings, M.D. The death of this venerable man, known to so many of the first settlers of Oneida county as an eminent physician, occurred in Clinton the 26 ult., in the 81st year of his age. For about ten years Mr. Hastings had been laid aside hy paralysis, which affected his limbs and speech. His long confinement was however a period of cheerful serenity - the calm close of a long Summer day - awaiting till his change should come. He succeeded his father in the practice of medicine in that community, and during the forty years of his active life was ever as ready and able to administer to spiritual as to physical necessities. His disposition was most social and kindly, and like his brother (the late Orlando Hastings, Esq., of Rochester), who went before him scarcely a week, he has been a pillar in the church.   5. [S213] Newspaper article from the Scrapbook/Album maintained by Caroline S. Hastings, page 17.     The Late Dr. Hastings. Died, in this village, on the 26th ult., Seth Hastings, M.D., aged 81 years. The decease of so important a citizen, calls for a more extended notice than the ordinary obituary record. Dr. Hastings was born in Washington, Ct., Aug. 23d 1780, and when seventeen years old, came with his parents to Clinton. His father before him was a physician of extensive practice. The son, having completed his academic and professional studies, was taken into partnership with his father, in the year 1801, he then being 21 years of age. The young doctor grew in public esteem, from year to year, until at length he became the leading physician of the place. His services were also much sought for in the adjoining towns of this county. Other physicians of high repute established themselves in Clinton, but Dr. Hastings, we believe, maintained the largest practice. After nearly fifty years of useful labor, he was afflicted with a stroke of paralysis, which rendered him almost speechless and physically helpless. From that time, until his death, a period of ten years, while deprived of nearly all social intercourse and personal enjoyment, he was watched over by the tenderest filial care, and supplied with everything needful for his comfort. At his funeral, in addition to the resident members of the family, many of his relations from abroad were present: two brothers, four sons, a son-in-law and a nephew. The funeral services were held in the Congregational church, and were conducted by the Pastor, and by Revs. Mr. Strong and Prof. Upson. The tributes which these gentlemen paid to the memory of the deceased were as deserved as they were interesting. Mr. Strong, in particular, spoke from a life-long acquaintance with Dr. H., and his manner showed that he spoke from the heart. On the Sabbath following his funeral, Rev. Mr. Swift preached an appropriate sermon with references to the deceased from the words: "Let me die the death of the righteous, and let my last end be like his."
         We can hardly add anything to the testimonials given on the above occasions; nor shall we attempt any labored analysis of this venerable man's character. Possessed of good native endowments, he cultivated them by life-long reading and observation. His social character was very marked. Amiable, cheerful, hopeful, with a smile and a kind word for everybody, he diffused happiness all around him. He loved to find the sunny side of life, and did much to make it sunny. This trait of character helped to render him an agreeable and successful physician. His house was the home of a generous hospitality. For many years, it was seldom without its guests from all parts of the country. He was particularly fond of social gathering in which music formed a leading part of the entertainment. On Thanksgiving evenings, for many successive years, his parlors were filled with family friends, old and young, of a musical turn, and the walls echoed with joyful singing. The tunes selected were old and new, long and short, half-forgotten and familiar, those with a decidedly ancient odor having the preference. On such occasions, Dr. Hastings seemed to be in his true element. He was leader of the choir in the Old White Meeting House, for several years; and until laid aside in the year 1851, he usually conducted the singing of the church on Communion Sabbaths, and at all social meetings.
         The religious character of Dr. Hastings, was not the least of his excellencies. Humble, prayerful, earnest, his piety showed itself in his household, in the prayer-meeting, amid his professional pursuits, and in all other walks of life. But of these things, so fully exhibited on the funeral occasion, we need not now further speak.
         It was a great loss to Clinton, when Dr. Hastings was withdrawn from public life. He was, from the first, one of the leading minds in this town, and did much to determine and mold its character. He was the friend of temperance, and order, morality, education and religion. He was eminently a peace-maker. He was interested in all good public enterprises, and helped them forward according to his ability. He did much to introduce new and improved varieties of fruits into this region, and set a good example in the way of ornamental gardening. But we need not prolong this sketch the subject of it will long live in the hearts of all who knew him. The memory of the just is blessed.
  6. [S207] Francis H. Hastings, Family Record of Dr. Seth Hastings Senior of Clinton New York, page 27 - 28. Dr. Seth Hastings, Jr. eldest son of Dr. Seth Hastings, born in Washington, Litchfield County, Conn., August 23, 1780. Died in Clinton, Oneida County, N. Y., March 26, 1861, aged 81 years.
         When he was seventeen years old he removed with his parents to Clinton, and having completed his academic and professional studies, he was taken into partnership with his father in the year 1801. For nearly fifty years he was the leading physician of the place, and he was often called to adjoining towns.
         He was, from the first, one of the leading minds in the community, and did much to determine and mold its character. He was the friend of temperance and order, morality, education, and religion. He was eminently a peacemaker. He was interested in all good public enterprises, and helped them forward according to his ability.
         His religious character was marked. Humble, prayerful, earnest, his piety showed itself in his household, in the prayer-meetings, amid his professional pursuits, and in all the relations of life. He was leader of the choir in the Old White Meeting House for several years, and until laid aside in 1851, by illness, he usually conducted the singing of the church on Communion Sabbaths and at all social meetings.
         Possessed of good native endowments, he cultivated them by life-long reading and observation. His was a social nature. Amiable, cheerful, hopeful, with a smile and a kind word for everybody, he diffused happiness all around him. He loved to find the sunny side of life, and he did much to make it sunny. This trait of character helped to make him an agreeable and successful physician.
         About the year 1808 he built the red brick house, which has been known for ninety years as the "Hastings Homestead." It was sold shortly after the death of Dr. Hastings, and within the past year it has been repaired, though its original plan has been preserved as far as possible, and it is now used as a bank. A recent paper read before the Rural Art Society, of Clinton, mentions the doorway and parlor mantlepiece of this house as being good examples of colonial art.
         This was a home of generous hospitality. For many years it was seldom without its guests from all parts of the country. Dr. Hastings was particularly fond of social gatherings in which music formed a leading part of the entertainment. On Thanksgiving evenings, for many successive years his parlors were filled with family friends, old and young, of a musical turn, and the walls echoed with joyful singing. The tunes selected were old and new, long and short, half-forgotten and familiar; those with a decidedly ancient odor having the preference. On such occasions Dr. Hastings seemed to be in his true element. It was been said that one "could seldom pass the old brick mansion without hearing either vocal or instrumental music, or both."
         His love of horticulture made his garden and orchard remarkable for that time. In that part known as the "Botanical Garden" was to be found nearly every native plant, and many others sent by correspondents from distant section of the country. He was constantly adding new varieties of fruit to his collection, and by careful selection of the more hardly kinds he secured a bountiful supply of fine fruit the entire year.
  7. [S213] Newspaper article from the Scrapbook/Album maintained by Caroline S. Hastings, page 26 and 28. Some recollections of Clinton and Vicinity. Fifth Paper. A Notable Clinton Family.
         The name of Hastings is historic. The reader of English history and literature has often met with it. As a locality it gave its name to one of the decisive battles of the world, in which the Saxon king Harold was slain, and William the Norman became master of England, and changed its civilization and destiny.
         The first and greatest governor of India was a Hastings, and in his biography Macauley says: "The undoubted splendor of the name of Hastings needs no illustration from fable. One branch of that line wore in the fourteenth century the coronet of Pembroke. From another branch sprang the renowned chamberlain, the faithful adherent of the White Rose, whose fate has furnished so striking a theme both to poets and historians." But it is no part of my purpose to show how, or by whom the name was made illustrious in old England. It was brought thither by Thomas Hastings and his wife, Susanna, who on the 10th of April, 1634, embarked at Ipswich in the good ship Elizabeth, to which in due time arrived at Boston. I do not know whether Thomas belonged to the aristocracy, but I suspect he was a good Puritan. He settled in Watertown, Mass., and he and Susanna gave the family of Hastings a good start in America with eight children. And here may be noted a striking difference between this and other noted families. All others, so far as I know, started with the emigration of three brothers, one of who settled in New England, one in New Jersey or Pennsylvania, and the third in the south. But fortunately, in the case of the Hastings family, there was but one. I say fortunately solely on the score of room, for if all who have borne this name, were like those it has been the writers good fortune to know, the county would have been no worse in other respects if the orthodox number of three had emigrated, and been equally prolific. I cannot of course show how, from this Thomas and his children the name of Hastings spread over the land. One family alone claims the writer's notice. Ten years after Moses Foote and company camped upon the ground now known as Clinton village, and after the settlement had been reinforced by sundry additions from the same state of Connecticut, a descendant of Thomas Hastings, Seth by name, a physician, thirty-three years of age, with his wife, Eunice Parmelee, whom he had married when she was only sweet sixteen, with the eight children that had been born to them in Connecticut, left Washington in that state for the new west. The fame of Clinton had become well-known in many a Connecticut town that had sent its contributions of sturdy men and lovely women to its vicinity, and thither the said Seth and family bent their steps. They settled on a farm about two miles south of Clinton, on the road to Paris Hill, and there the doctor and his wife remained until their death, the wife dying in 1821, and the doctor in 1830, at the good old age of 85.
         To this couple were born in Clinton three more children, making a total of eleven. The list is as follows:
         Seth, born August 23, 1780. This was the head of the well-known family in the village, who following the profession of his father, studying and practicing with him for some years. At the age of twenty-two he married Huldah Clark, daughter of John and Anne Clark, who had emigrated from Colchester, Conn., in 1797. This couple are the hero and heroine of our story, but we leave them for a little and continue the list of Seth, senior.
         Betsy, born August 28, 1782, became the wife of Doctor Elnathan Judd, of Paris Hill, and the mother of Doctor Gerrett Judd, who in 1827, married Laura Fish, a teacher in Miss Royce's school, and in the same year went as missionary to the Sandwich Islands. He afterwards became very conspicuous in the history of the Islands. Mrs. Betsy Judd after the death of her husband, went to the Islands and died at her son's in 1876.
         Thomas, born October 15, 1784. This was the celebrated musical teacher, composer and poet, and whilom leader of the village choir. He died May 15, 1872, leaving behind him a name dear to the Christian church. There are in the well known "Songs of the Sanctuary," nineteen tunes and twenty-seven hymns composed by him. Rev. Thos. S. Hastings, a graduate of Hamilton college, class of 1848, and now a professor in the Union Theological seminary, of New York, is his son.
         Eunice, born October 22, 1786, died January 10, 1788.
         Orlando, born March 7, 1789, died March 19, 1861. He, with the next on the list, was once a merchant in Clinton, afterwards a prominent lawyer in Rochester, N.Y.
         Eurotas P., born July 20, 1791, died June 1, 1886, a banker in Detroit, Mich. The tune "Detroit," found in the "Songs of the Sanctuary," was composed by him.
         Charles, born September 2, 1793, died March 28, 1848. He kept a book and stationery store in Utica for some years, and afterwards was a Michigan farmer.
         Polly Sophia, born April 14, 1796, died June 10, 1803.
         Truman, born August 29, 1798, died October 17, 1884. A lawyer, first at Rochester, N.Y., afterwards at Cleveland, O. Thomas, Orlando and Truman, were albinos.
         Albert M., born July 16, 1804, died October 4, 1828. A merchant in Niagara county, N.Y.
         Eunice Sophia, born Sept. 22, 1809, died October 14, 1849. Married Dr. W. Smith, who died a little over a year after. Married again in 1840 to Levi Trowbridge, of Michigan.
         It is time to return to the tribe of Dr. Seth, the second. To him and his wife Huldah were born in Clinton fifteen children, all of whom, save one, reached the age of maturity. The list is as follows:
         John Clark, born June 10, 1803, died August 28, 1880. He married Martha Parker, of Utica, where he was for some years a well-known merchant. As all Clinton people well know, he was afterwards a nurseryman and landscape gardener in Clinton. Many places in Clinton, especially the college grounds and the cemetery, are proofs of his skill and good taste.
         Sophia, born May 26, 1805, died Nov. 28, 1876. Became the wife of Rev. A.S. Wells, a well-known pioneer missionary in Michigan.
         George, born March 13, 1807, died August 29, 1866, a respected and successful lawyer of Mount Morris, N.Y. A graduate of Hamilton college, class of '26.
         Juliet, born December 18, 1808, died August 30, 1848, became the wife of Rev. Wayne Gridley, of Clinton, five years pastor of the Congregationalist church.
         Seth Merwin, born March 14, 1810, died in infancy.
         Daniel H., born April 20, 1811, died February 24, 1884. Was a merchant and afterwards a physician in New York city.
         Seth P.M., born April 21, 1813, died February 24, 1876. Was a minister settled in Ulster county, N.Y. Married Elizabeth Buttrick, of Clinton, who also died in Ulster county. Was a graduate of Hamilton college, class of '33
         Francis H., born November 23, 1814. I am happy to say that "uncle Frank" still lives. He tried for some years to make trees grow at Bridgeport, Chicago; gave it up and sold out and became a fruit gardener, near Rochester, N.Y. Married Amelia, daughter of the late Hon. Daniel Gott, of Syracuse, N.Y.
         Panett M., born August 17, 1816. Is a practicing physician at Hartford, Conn., where he married Jane Sheldon. Was a graduate of Hamilton college, class of '38, and was for two years one of the "praelectores" of the college.
         Mary Alida, born March 11, 1818. Married James Sibley, a prominent merchant of Cincinnati, O.
         Elizabeth Ann, born September 27, 1819, died March 2, 1863.
         Eurotas P., born April 17, 1821. Is a minister and missionary at Ceylon. Married Anne Cleveland, a sister of President Cleveland. A graduate of Hamilton college, class of '42.
         Caroline S., born February 17, 1823, lives at Cincinnati.
         Francis E., born June 7, 1825, died April 8, 1875.
         Edward A., born May 28, 1828, and still living at Clinton, completes the chapter. The mantle of the oldest in the list has fallen upon the youngest.
         I well remember when the last named was born, having that morning called as usual at the Hastings mansion for company to the old Academy. Seth was, or pretended to be, greatly disgusted with this addition to the catalog. But he did not pout long and it is not to be supposed that his playing mugwump had any influence in closing the list at No. 15. The old doctor wore a broader smile than usual.
         But it is not on the score of numbers alone that this family has been selected for notice in these sketches. It was a most remarkable family in its morals. In neither of the two flocks of Seth was there a black sheep. The writer, first and last, has been somewhat acquainted with their history, and never heard of anything disreputable of any member. As has been noticed it has embraced merchants, bankers, horticulturists, lawyers, ministers, missionaries and professors.
         It is one of the mysteries how Dr. Hastings, with the moderate fees of a country practitioner of that day, could bring up such a family and give them all a good education, and make for them such a pleasant and comfortable home. Verily the old sulky did not rest much. I suppose that all were Christians. What a family will that be in heaven!
         Dr. Hastings was in some respects a remarkable man. His temperament was happy, cheerful, and often mirthful. It must have been a blue day indeed when his face did nor wear a smile. Often did it fall to the lot of the writer to see him open his saddlebags, or his little chest, and dose out his pills and powders, with a smile that was better than sugar-coating, or other art of modern pharmacy. He was eminently a peace-maker in church and society, and his heart always seemed to be full of that charity which "suffereth long and is kind."
         I have spoken of Thomas Hastings as the singer, &c. But all the Hastings' I think, were addicted to music. It was a family gift. One could seldom pass the old brick mansion without hearing either vocal or instrumental music, or both. Dr. Hastings was also noted for his love of horticulture. He always had the finest garden, shrubbery and fruits in the place, and within and without there was no more attractive home in old Clinton.
         What his sons and grandsons and his brothers have done to perpetuate the honored name of Hastings, it is no part of my purpose to record.
         If it were allowable to conjecture that the Lord God had appeared to old Thomas of England and said: "Get thee out of thy country and from thy kindred and from thy father's house, unto a land that I will show thee, and I will make of thee a great nation." surely enough has been recorded to show that he is still the same covenant-keeping God that he was in the days of Abraham. Geo. H. Woodruff. Joliet, Ill., Feb. 16.

Hastings, page 36.      Seth Hastings, Jr., the oldest son, became like his father, a physician, and settled in Clinton. For some forty years he enjoyed a very successful practice, rarely if ever exceeded in respect and profit in any of the country districts of central New York. Other sons were distinguished in different lines of business. Of his daughters two became wives of clergymen, another the wife of a well-known business man of Cincinnati, all of them parents of sons and daughters too numerous to mention, none of whom, to my knowledge, have ever disgraced their parentage.
         Thomas Hastings, the sweet singer in our Israel, editor for some years of the religious paper published in Utica, N.Y., since more widely known as musical professor in New York city, was the second son. His work is too well known, his reputation, with that of his excellent wife, is too well established to need any word of eulogy from me. Their two daughters became wives of eminent missionaries, and have gone to their rest, while their only son, the Rev. Thomas S. Hastings, D.D., pastor of the West church, New York city, is left as the lone, yet worthy representative of that branch of the family.
         Orlando Hastings, the eminent counselor for many late years, until his death well known as a leader in the Central Presbyterian church of Rochester, was the third son. His standing as a lawyer, as a Christian, can be attested by nearly all Rochester, and certainly by all connected with the Central church. His son, the Rev. Parsons C. Hastings, of Brooklyn, is well known. Another of the sons of Dr. Seth Hastings, Sen., was the late Eurotas P. Hastings, of Detroit. His career from his clerkship with Ephraim Hart, in Utica, N.Y., through his cashiership with Dr. Henry Dwight, banker in Geneva and in Detroit, during his presidency of the old bank of Michigan, and as a prominent Christian citizen and an officer in Dr. Duffield's church, is an unbroken history of success of honor and usefulness.
         Charles Hastings, the father of the late Rev. Albert E. Hastings, pastor of the Union Presbyterian church in Detroit, was another son of this remarkable family. He was for many years well known as the firm of Merrell & Hastings, booksellers in Utica, N.Y. This firm were the publishers of the Religious Newspaper, edited by Thomas Hastings. On the dissolution of the firm, some forty-five years since, he removed to Troy, Mich., when, as more conducive to his health, [ ] had become impaired, he promoted [ ] pursuits maintaining in his modest, retiring, yet useful life, as he had ever before done, the reputation of a sincere and devout Christian gentleman. The character and usefulness of his son, the late Albert E. Hastings, is a testimony to the excellence and fidelity of his parents. That the death of the son is mourned by a "wide circle of friends and admires" is readily believed by those who knew those parents.
         Truman Hastings, the last to be mentioned of the sons, I believe, is yet living. He was associated many years with his brother Orlando in the practice of law, and of established merit, although he was, of course, overshadowed by the prominence of his brother.
         It only remains to speak of the daughter. Betsey Hastings married Dr. Elnathan Judd. They were the parents of Dr. Gerrit P. Judd, who more than fifty years since went to the Sandwich Islands as a missionary-physician under the care of the American board. After serving several years in that capacity, so marked with his ability and so well fitted was he thought to be for the highest civil service in the state, that he was released from his service to the board that he might accept the invitation of the king to become his secretary of state or prime minister. During a long course of honorable service in this capacity he was eminently useful in guiding the affairs of that infant state, ever mindful of the high interests of virtue and religion. During his long official career in the interests of the king he twice visited Great Britain and the United States, taking with him the princess, heir-apparent to the throne, to make them personally acquainted with the condition of these enlightened Christian nations.
         A daughter of Dr. Judd, the elder, and Mrs. Judd, was married to Mr. Bates, a lawyer of eminence, who also went to the Sandwich Islands and served the king in an official capacity. After the death of her husband, Mrs. Betsey Judd, with an unmarried daughter, also went to the Sandwich Islands and spent the remainder of her days enjoying the prosperity of the people to which her children had so largely contributed, and participating with them in the respect and honors they had so richly merited. She lived to pass her ninetieth birthday which was celebrated by a remarkable party, to which all the aged grandmothers of Honolulu were invited. Three of these sons of Mr. Hastings, Sen., were Albinos, which should be considered in forming our estimate of their usefulness. In this was found probably the severest test of the faith of their parents. To them the question, how can these almost blind and helpless ones, gain a living. Their anxiety on this account was well known in the circle of their intimate friends. Their only resource was to the loving care and providence of him who had ordered their infirmity, and richly was their confidence rewarded.
         I have written this sketch, not that any one of these persons named excelled all others, but because taken all together their history will rarely find a parallel illustrating that benefit of a sound Christian education, and the riches of divine grace in the fulfillment of covenant promises. It was my privilege to know quite intimately this entire circle, and I can testify to their eminent position and usefulness in their several spheres, and wish to say in conclusion that I regarded their friendship among the richest blessings of my social life.

One was the cheeriest, merriest little old gentleman. His face was always rippling with smiles. He was deacon; and on communion Sabbaths (the profane Devil dispensed with) he stood in front of the pulpit and sang Dundee or Mear, and tried to look solemn. As I think of him, standing there, with his round sunny head and his round sunny face, he comes up in my memory like those pictures of Sol in the old almanacs. But the other was a tall, sharp-faced, somber, unsympathetic man; and he was "our doctor." I think I should weigh fifty pounds more today if I had had the other doctor. My childhood was loomed over by that sunless, comfortless, excellent, wise man, who cured me, but never cheered me. There is physic in the bright faces and the joyous, bracing, tender tones of some people. Dr. Seth Hastings, Jr., was the merry one.

    The death of J. C. Hastings, the landscape gardener in charge of the work of laying out the extensive grounds of the Fiske property, occurred at one o'clock this afternoon of pneumonia, at the residence of Mrs. J. W. Williams, on the campus. Mr. Hastings was taken with a severe cold on Thursday last, but he continued to attend to his duties and refused to have a doctor, remarking that he did not think he needed one. He grew worse and was finally obliged to take to his room. Yesterday he was visited by Architect W. H. Miller who was surprised and alarmed to find him suffering from pneumonia. The Architect insisted upon having a physician called and Mr. Hastings eventually consented. It was written by Mr. Wells but a few weeks previous to his death, and will be recognized as characteristic of the man, and we know will prove interesting to his many friends in Iowa. The funeral was held at the Congregational church Wednesday last, Rev. M.E. Dwight conducting the services. The remains were interred in the Evergreen Cemetery.
    Editors Ledger: In accordance with your request I have prepared, and now submit to your disposal, the outlines of my life, as one of the octogenarians of Jefferson county. I find it no easy matter to give within the proper limits of a newspaper article the prominent figures and facts of a life protracted to near the close of 84 years; and I shrink from the attempt lest it should savor of ostentation. But I am a witness for Christ, and desire to give my testimony to the world of what His free grace has done for me, who in my early life was the chief of sinners, and ever since a very unprofitable servant.
         I am the eldest son of Shipley and Dorothea Wells, and was born in Jericho, Vermont, Dec. 3d, 1798. When about 15 years old, in answer to the prayers of a pious mother, I was born again in Washington, Me., and united with the Congregational church. In 1819-1824 I entered and graduated at Hamilton College, in Clinton, Oneida county, N.Y. There, in 1828, I married Sophia Hastings, daughter of Dr. Seth and Hulda Hastings, having previously taught the academy in Detroit for two years and taken a short course of theological study at Auburn. Having a passion for the conversion of sinners and for the work of home evangelization in our western states, I was ordained to this work by the Presbytery of Oneida, at Utica, in February of this year, and as the pioneer of the Western Fraternity in Auburn, and under the commission of the A.H.M.S., went forth with my wife to New Albany, Ind., eight hundred miles from her father's home. We were joyfully received by the members of a feeble Presbyterian church, having five male members, only one of whom could lead in prayer, and fifteen ladies, who sustained a female prayer meeting and a small Sabbath school. The only place for holding religious meetings was a small gloomy court-house, and although a place of nearly two thousand inhabitants, had only occasional preaching; and a small M.E. church, whose house of worship was only bare walls covered with a roof, and its membership scarcely stronger than our own. Intemperance was fearfully prevalent, as whisky was in every store, shop and steamboat, as free as water for customers, friends and workmen. All the resistance to stay this tide of death and ruin was in these feeble churches, most of whose male members sanctioned the use of intoxicating drinks themselves.
         Here I began my home missionary work, and for four years labored to win souls to Christ; and through His blessings upon the word, and means of grace employed by myself and others, we had erected a good brick church and paid for it from our own means, gathered a good congregation, a large Sabbath-school, organized a vigorous temperance society and added one hundred and fifty to our membership, so that the whole moral character of the place was changed. The revivals we enjoyed during those four years laid the foundation for its commercial prosperity and its religious improvement to this day. There are now in that city three large and well established churches, and after fifty years have passed away there are in all of them valued members still holding forth the word of life who were brought into christian fellowship during that season of grace.
         Leaving New Albany in 1832, I settled in Tecumseh, Mich., and became pastor of the Presbyterian church there, and the first that was ever installed in that state. Here, too, I was remarkably favored with revivals, one of which continued in some of our preaching places for two years. Under their influence churches were organized in Clinton and Racine, and the way prepared to organize them in three other places. Numbers of valuable members were gathered into them, both by examination and letters. Houses of worship have since been erected, and these congregations are now the light and salt of those localities.
         In 1836-40, similar results were secured in Troy, Oakland county, and in the neighboring towns of Rochester, Birmingham and Wing Lake; and in Mt. Clemens in another four year's pastorate, from 1840 to 1844. From 1844 to 1850 I traveled over the state of Michigan and northern Indiana, and a part of Illinois and Wisconsin, in the service of the A.B.C.F. missions, and for six years labored to promote the cause of foreign missions in all the Presbyterian and Congregational churches in that district. I then resumed my home missionary work, and for five years labored in the churches of Lima, Ontario and Springfield, in Indiana, and in Sturges Prairie, in Michigan. This field of labor, although not barren of good fruits, was not as fruitful as those I had cultivated before.
         In 1850 I removed to Iowa, and for the last twenty-seven years my home and center of operations has been in Fairfield. From this point I went forth for several years and labored in the destitute churches in this state and in Illinois until I was laid aside on account of a diseased throat (laryngitis). Since then I have canvassed Jefferson county, and mainly by personal visitation have supplied all who would receive the scriptures, and raised funds to pay off a debt that at one time amounted to nearly seven hundred dollars. I have endeavored in Fairfield to be in all things a "faithful witness" for Christ. And now on the borders of the grave and eternity, I feel happy in all the testimony I have given against slavery, intemperance, the use of tobacco, secret societies, and the use of alcoholic wine in the Lord's Supper, and in favor of the Bible as the word of God and the blessedness of the service of Jesus Christ. I wish to leave it as my last testimony that He who saved me is able and willing to save to the uttermost all who will enthrone Him in their hearts, and assure them that they will find, as I have, that "Godliness is profitable unto all things having promise of the life that now is and of that which is to come." My life work and my prayers I leave as a heritage for my beloved children and friends; and praying that they and you may follow me as I have.

         The following account of Col. Hastings' war record is taken from a "History of the Class of 1857, of Hamilton college:"
         On August 19, 1862, he obtained from the Governor of New York authority to recruit a company for the Twenty-fourth New York Independent Battery of Light Artillery, then in the field. He very quickly secured the enlistment of over eight men for that battery, among whom were many college men.
         "The country was never more patriotic, and yielded the flower of her youth at that time for the sacred cause of Liberty and Union. It was hard to leave wife and home, and in doing so he was actuated by a controlling sense of duty. He was in active service until January, 1865, first with the rank of First Lieutenant, and finally receiving a commission as Brevet-Colonel. For over a year he served as Judge-Advocate of the sub-District of the Albemarle, in North Carolina, and participated in several battles.
         "On April 20, 1864, while on duty at Plymouth, N. C., with a small brigade, numbering about two thousand men, he was captured after a series of engagements, commencing on April 17, and resulting finally, after repeated repulses of the enemy, in a grand charge over our earthworks and defenses, and the capture of the entire garrison after several hours' fighting with the defenses. He was held as prisoner of war for nearly six months. At this period of the war exchanges of prisoners had ceased. He made four attempts to escape; the first was by means of a tunnel constructed with infinite labor during the night time, the only implements being a pair of hands, a broken gate-hinge and a jack-knife. The period of construction was six weeks, length of tunnel forty feet, ventilation imperfect, caliber just sufficient for one person who has subsisted on Confederate diet for six months.
         The second scheme resulted in the escape of all engaged in it, with the exception of Colonel Hastings. The third attempt was successful in a limited and disappointing sense; he traveled for four nights through the heart of Georgia, and was finally held up by one John Jackson, a Georgia planter, aided by three hounds and one black man. His possession of a map of the country was regarded as positive proof of his being a spy. He was sent in irons to the city of Savannah, held for a few hours in the cell of a jail, but was finally restored to the so-called rights of a prisoner of war.
         "On October 11, 1864, with four New York officers, he succeeded in running the guard-line of a military prison camp in Columbia, S. C. After thirty-four days of adventure, with constant vigils and many narrow escapes from capture, the entire party reached the Union lines at an outpost near Knoxville, Tenn.
         "As he was making preparations to return to the field he received a telegram from Governor Fenton, which resulted in his appointment, first, as military secretary of the Governor of New York, and shortly afterward as private secretary.
         "He resided in the city of Albany from January, 1865, to September, 1868, at which time he resigned and removed to the city of New York, resuming the practice of his profession. He now resides in Morristown, N. J., having offices at No. 265 Broadway, New York. He is the senior member of the law firm of Hastings & Gleason."

Wednesday afternoon, 15th, our community was shocked at the announcement of the death of Mrs. Julia, wife of Miles B. McNair of this town, aged 42 years, after a brief and severe illness. She was daughter of the late Judge Hastings, and was born in this village in May, 1839. She was a woman of rare traits of character, intelligent, quiet, untiring and persistent in her daily duties, devoted to her family and friends, and supremely unselfish. Her duties performed had their daily round, ever evincing under every trial the same sweet uncomplaining disposition, and earnest devotion to those about her. The discipline of life had indeed perfected her transparent and beautiful character, as was said by one at her funeral, 'her life could be summed up in two words, unselfishness and fidelity.' Truly her life was interwoven in the lives of those about her, and no wife, mother, or friend ever illustrated more fully and delightfully the christian graces. She was a faithful member of the Presbyterian Church, and a constant attendant, and no weariness could keep her from every Sabbath gathering her infant class, as with remarkable simplicity she unfolded to them the simple stories of the holy word. Her death has cast a dark shadow over many a heart, while her gentle ways, kindness of heart, disinterestedness, and pure love, have left their sweet fragrance in many a household. To mourn her sudden and unexpected departure she leaves a family of seven children, whose correct training indicate the sure reward of maternal fidelity; a husband, mother, four brothers, George S., of New York, Edward H., of Bath, Fred E., of Little Rock, Arkansas, and John M., of this village; two sisters, Kittie of this village, and Mrs. Thalheimer of Cincinnati, Ohio; and a large circle of friends.
