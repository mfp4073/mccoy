The will of John Wright of Kelvedon Arch; Essex. Register Thomder 117 and Register Bastwick. (Both copies examined.) In the name of God Amen; 25 Sept, 1551, of Kellydon Essex, Yoeman. Beying in bode feble and weak yet of pfect mynde make my will, etc. Soul to God. Body to be buried within the Chancell of Kellydon Church. To poor mens box 20/-. To the parish of Southweld 40/- and to the ten poorest parishes about me 20/- each. To my loving wife (not named) 40 marks by the yere to be paid of my lands by my four sons equally - £6, 13, 4 each. And the best end of my house of Kelvedon Haule at her choice. Also to her my household stuff and £100, 20 kyne and a gelding. To each daughter (not named ) £13, 6, 8. To every godchild 6/8, and to every godchild of my own childrens 20/-. To Kelydon Strete toward the reparation of the way £3, 6, 8. To mending the way to the Common £3, 6, 8. To the reparation of Geedy Hall Lane 40/-. To my eldest son John Wright and his heirs Kelydon Hall, Weldsyde Knights, Hubbords Land with the two houses in Brentwood and Layndon. To Robert Wright and his heirs, Ropers in Brook Street, and house meadow and orchard in Brook Street, Smythes lands, Powres wherein not gladden dwelleth with Fyners, Burnecks Land and Webbs. To my son called Myddle John I give all the land I have in Havering and houses and millers house and a tenement in childerditch wherin Gibbs doth dwell. To my son called Young John I give Bishops Hall, Wilchins Pownde Mead, Welde Lyes, Bulffandes and Dichars in Ramsden Bellhouse and Trays. To John Wright son of my son John the younger, all my land in Navestock. To John Wright son of my son John the elder, a tenement called Drywoods. To John Wright son of my son Robert, a tenement called Argents. To every child (if any) of my childrens children 20/- apeace. To each servant 6/8. Executors: Sons John the elder and Robert. Overseers: Sons, middle and young John (note middle John is here styled "John Wright of Wrights Bridge"). Witnesses: Thomas Wood, clerk (I.E. parson) Robert Sheparde, John Symode, and Thomas Lytman. Proved 21 November 1551 at Stapleford Abbottts by the two Executors named. (Register Bastwick states it was proved at Brentwood but agrees in the date.).

  1. [S565] Source not known, These are the stories that were told to me about William J. Smith (Uncle Bill), who married my aunt, Audrey Thyson:

    He was orphaned at a fairly young age and went to live with an older brother and sister-in-law in San Francisco. Apparently, he did not like his sister-in-law much. One day when he was down on the docks, a sailor on a ship called down, "Hey, son, are you an ordinary seaman?" Uncle Bill said, "Yes," though, of course, he wasn't, and signed onto the ship as a crew member. We think that he said he was sixteen but wasn't. He then sailed around the world. He later told us that when he got to someplace interesting, he would jump ship. I think that he hitchhiked across the outback of Australia and worked in the gold mines of South Africa. When he had seen enough of a country, he returned to port and signed onto another ship.

    After he had returned to the States, he signed onto an Alaskan fishing boat called the Star of Alaska. Around this time he joined the Seaman's Union and became interested in the rights of the working man. This led to a lifetime career in the labor unions. He eventually became a director or a vice-president of the AFL-CIO in charge of the Southwest Region of the U.S. He met my aunt Aud when she was writing for a labor newspaper. Uncle Bill had been married once before and had a daughter named Joan.

    Note: The ship, the Star of Alaska, was later docked in San Francisco and polished up as a tourist attraction. When Uncle Bill was touring it with one of his daughters, he remarked that it was just like the ship that he had sailed to Alaska on. Then he read the sign about the history of the ship and said that it WAS the ship that he had sailed on.

    This entry was made by Janice (Brown) Benham on January 4, 2000.
